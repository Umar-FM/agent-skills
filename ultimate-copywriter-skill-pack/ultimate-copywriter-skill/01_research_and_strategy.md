# 01 — Research, Market Diagnosis, Positioning, and Strategy

This module teaches the agent to think before writing. Copy quality is limited by research quality. The goal is not to collect facts for their own sake; the goal is to find the message that makes the buyer feel understood, see the value, trust the proof, and act.

## Research mindset

Research is the act of reducing guesswork. Do not ask, “What can we say?” Ask, “What is already true in the buyer's world that the copy should make visible?”

Great research finds:

- What buyers say when they are not being marketed to.
- What they tried before.
- What triggered the search.
- What they fear will happen if they choose wrong.
- What language they use for the problem.
- What makes the promise believable or unbelievable.
- What alternatives frame the buying decision.
- What internal politics or personal stakes are involved.
- What evidence would help them persuade themselves or others.

A copywriter without research writes slogans. A copywriter with research writes decisions.

## Research hierarchy

Use the strongest available source first.

1. **Live customer conversations** — interviews, sales calls, support calls, onboarding calls.
2. **Actual buying behavior** — CRM data, funnel analytics, demo requests, churn reasons, expansion reasons.
3. **Voice-of-customer artifacts** — reviews, testimonials, survey answers, support tickets, chat logs, community posts, cancellation feedback.
4. **Sales and success team knowledge** — recurring objections, deal stories, product usage, implementation reality.
5. **Competitor and category analysis** — landing pages, reviews, ads, pricing pages, positioning, claims.
6. **Search and social behavior** — search queries, questions, forums, comments, creator content, community language.
7. **Internal assumptions** — useful only when labeled and tested.

Never treat internal assumptions as equal to buyer language.

The final output of research should be a message map, not a pile of notes.


## Customer interview method

Use customer interviews to uncover buying language, not to pitch.

### Interview rules

- Ask about the past, not imagined future behavior.
- Ask for stories, not opinions.
- Listen for exact phrases.
- Ask follow-up questions until the language becomes concrete.
- Do not ask leading questions.
- Separate user, buyer, influencer, and approver when relevant.
- Ask what almost stopped the purchase.
- Ask what changed that made the timing urgent.

### Interview opening

> Thanks for making time. I am not here to sell you anything. I am trying to understand what was happening before you looked for a solution, how you compared options, and what made the decision feel worth it. Specific stories are more useful than polished answers.

### Core interview questions

#### Situation and trigger

- Take me back to when you first realized this was a problem. What was happening?
- What triggered the search for a solution?
- Was there a specific incident, deadline, metric, complaint, cost, or leadership request?
- What had you already tried?
- Why did those attempts not solve it?

#### Pain and stakes

- What was most frustrating about the old way?
- What did the problem cost you in time, money, risk, reputation, confidence, or team morale?
- What happened if you did nothing?
- Who else felt the pain?
- How did this show up in your day-to-day work?

#### Alternatives

- What other solutions did you consider?
- What did you like about those options?
- What made you hesitate?
- What did you compare us against that we might not think of as a competitor?
- Did you consider solving it internally?

#### Decision criteria

- What had to be true for you to feel comfortable buying?
- What evidence mattered most?
- What questions did your team ask before approving it?
- Who needed to be convinced?
- What nearly stopped the deal?

#### Outcome

- What changed after using it?
- What result surprised you?
- What would you miss most if you no longer had it?
- How would you explain the value to a peer?
- What phrase would you use to describe this to someone with the same problem?

#### Messaging language

- What words would you use to describe the problem?
- What words feel overused or unbelievable in this category?
- What claims from vendors make you skeptical?
- What would make you think, “This was built for someone like me”?

### Follow-up prompts

Use these whenever the answer is abstract:

- What did that look like in practice?
- Can you give me an example?
- What happened next?
- How often did that happen?
- Who was involved?
- What did that cost?
- What did you say internally?
- What did you search for?
- What made that annoying?
- Why did that matter?

### Bad interview questions and better replacements

Bad:

> Would you buy a tool that saves time?

Better:

> Tell me about the last time this process took longer than expected. What happened?

Bad:

> Do you care about automation?

Better:

> Which parts of this process are still manual? Which ones are painful enough that you would pay to remove them?

Bad:

> What features do you want?

Better:

> What job were you trying to get done when the current system failed?

Bad:

> Is our product easy to use?

Better:

> Where did you get stuck the first time you tried to use it?

## Voice-of-customer mining

VOC mining converts raw customer language into copy assets.

### Sources to mine

- Customer reviews.
- Competitor reviews.
- App store reviews.
- Amazon reviews for adjacent products.
- Reddit, forums, Slack communities, Discord communities.
- Sales call transcripts.
- Support tickets.
- Chatbot logs.
- Survey responses.
- Cancellation forms.
- Onboarding questionnaires.
- Social comments.
- Search queries and autocomplete.
- Customer success notes.

### VOC tagging system

Tag every useful quote with one or more labels:

- `pain_current_state`
- `desired_outcome`
- `trigger_event`
- `failed_alternative`
- `objection_price`
- `objection_time`
- `objection_trust`
- `objection_complexity`
- `objection_fit`
- `buying_criterion`
- `proof_needed`
- `emotional_language`
- `functional_language`
- `category_language`
- `competitor_comparison`
- `aha_moment`
- `testimonial_seed`
- `headline_seed`
- `faq_seed`

### VOC transformation examples

Raw quote:

> “I was tired of hunting through Slack to figure out who approved what.”

Copy use:

> Stop hunting through Slack for the approval that should have been attached to the project.

Raw quote:

> “We had reports, but nobody trusted them because the numbers were always stale.”

Copy use:

> Reports are only useful if the team trusts the number before the decision is due.

Raw quote:

> “I did not want another platform the reps would ignore.”

Copy use:

> No new tab for reps to babysit. The coaching notes appear inside the workflow they already use.

Raw quote:

> “I needed something I could explain to my CFO in one slide.”

Copy use:

> Build the one-slide business case your CFO can approve without a second meeting.

## Jobs-to-be-done research

A buyer “hires” a product to make progress in a situation. The job includes functional, emotional, and social dimensions.

### JTBD formula

> When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [desired progress].

Examples:

> When a strategic account goes quiet before renewal, I want to know which risk signals appeared in recent calls, so I can intervene before the renewal becomes a discount negotiation.

> When I publish a weekly newsletter, I want a reliable way to turn one idea into multiple useful posts, so I can stay visible without spending every day writing from scratch.

> When my team hands work to a client, I want approvals captured in one place, so I can prevent revision loops and protect project margins.

### Functional, emotional, and social jobs

Functional:

> Reconcile invoice exceptions before close.

Emotional:

> Stop feeling anxious that an error will surface after reporting.

Social:

> Look prepared and credible in the finance meeting.

Copy should often speak to all three.

Example:

> Catch invoice exceptions before close, walk into the finance meeting with reconciled numbers, and stop worrying that one hidden mismatch will derail the report.

## Segmentation

Do not segment only by demographics or firmographics. Segment by buying situation, pain intensity, maturity, and desired outcome.

### Useful B2B segmentation dimensions

- Company size.
- Industry.
- Team structure.
- Maturity stage.
- Current tool stack.
- Volume of work.
- Urgency of problem.
- Regulatory or risk exposure.
- Budget owner.
- Implementation capacity.
- Buying committee complexity.
- Trigger event.

### Useful consumer segmentation dimensions

- Situation or life stage.
- Current habit.
- Desired identity.
- Pain intensity.
- Previous attempts.
- Budget sensitivity.
- Skepticism level.
- Usage occasion.
- Category familiarity.
- Social context.

### Segment examples

Weak segment:

> Small businesses.

Strong segment:

> Founder-led B2B service firms with 5-25 employees that depend on referrals but need a repeatable outbound and content system before hiring a full-time marketer.

Weak segment:

> Busy parents.

Strong segment:

> Parents of children under 6 who want healthy weeknight meals but have less than 20 minutes between daycare pickup and dinner.

## Ideal customer profile

An ICP should describe who gets disproportionate value and why.

```markdown
# ICP

## Best-fit customer
[Specific description]

## Pain intensity
[Why the problem is urgent]

## Ability to buy
[Budget, authority, timing]

## Ability to succeed
[Resources, context, maturity]

## Trigger events
[What makes now urgent]

## Exclusions
[Who is not a good fit]

## Messaging implications
[What the copy must emphasize]
```

Example:

> Best-fit customer: B2B SaaS companies with 20-100 sales reps, recorded sales calls, defined pipeline stages, and managers who coach but cannot review enough calls manually.
>
> Pain intensity: Deals stall after discovery because objection patterns are invisible until the quarter review.
>
> Ability to buy: VP Sales or Revenue Enablement owns the budget.
>
> Ability to succeed: Uses a CRM and call recorder; has a documented sales process.
>
> Exclusions: Teams without recorded calls or managers willing to coach.
>
> Messaging implication: Emphasize rep-specific coaching, not generic conversation intelligence.

## Buyer committee mapping

Many B2B purchases involve multiple stakeholders. Copy must equip the champion to persuade the committee.

### Stakeholder map

| Stakeholder | Cares about | Fears | Proof needed | Message angle |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| User | Ease, speed, workflow fit | More admin | Demo, screenshots | “Fits the way you already work.” |
| Manager | Performance, visibility | Low adoption | Team metrics, manager workflow | “See what is stuck and who needs support.” |
| Executive | ROI, risk, strategy | Waste, distraction | Business case, benchmark | “Reduce revenue leakage without adding headcount.” |
| Finance | Cost, payback | Unclear value | ROI model, terms | “Start with a scoped pilot.” |
| IT/Security | Integration, data | Risk, compliance | Security docs, architecture | “Integrates cleanly and controls access.” |
| Legal/Compliance | Liability | Exposure | Terms, audit trail | “Transparent controls and auditability.” |

### Champion enablement copy

Give the champion copy they can forward internally.

Example:

> I think this is worth evaluating because it addresses the handoff issue we discussed in QBR. The pilot is scoped to one team, does not require a CRM migration, and would show us whether missed next steps are contributing to deal slippage. The key question is whether the coaching insights are specific enough for managers to use weekly.

## Awareness-stage research

For every audience, determine awareness. Then adjust message depth.

### Unaware research questions

- What symptoms appear before they name the problem?
- What do they blame instead?
- What hidden cost can be made visible?
- What belief must shift?

Example copy:

> You may not have a hiring problem. You may have a knowledge-transfer problem that makes every new hire dependent on your busiest people.

### Problem-aware research questions

- How do they describe the pain?
- What have they tried?
- What makes the problem urgent?
- What happens if it continues?

Example copy:

> If every new hire asks the same ten questions, your documentation exists but your retrieval system is broken.

### Solution-aware research questions

- Which categories do they compare?
- What tradeoffs do they expect?
- What mechanisms do they believe in?
- Which claims feel tired?

Example copy:

> Unlike a static wiki, this builds role-based answer paths around the moments new hires actually get stuck.

### Product-aware research questions

- Why have they not bought yet?
- What proof is missing?
- What implementation fears remain?
- What would make the next step safer?

Example copy:

> Start with one role. We migrate the essential docs, map the first 30-day path, and show usage data before you expand.

### Most-aware research questions

- What final incentive or reminder matters?
- What deadline is real?
- What exact next step is easiest?

Example copy:

> Enroll by Friday to include the onboarding audit before your first build session.

## Market sophistication research

Ask:

- How many competitors make similar promises?
- What claims are overused?
- What mechanisms are common?
- What proof is now table stakes?
- What skepticism has the market developed?
- What deeper belief can be challenged?

### Sophistication examples

Low sophistication:

> Build your first email list.

Moderate sophistication:

> Build an email list of buyers, not freebie collectors.

High sophistication:

> The list-growth system that filters out freebie hunters before they ever join your funnel.

Very high sophistication:

> Stop chasing list size. Build the smallest audience that can reliably fund your next launch.

## Competitive research

Competitor research is not for copying. It is for finding sameness, whitespace, table stakes, proof gaps, and differentiation.

### Competitor analysis grid

| Competitor | Category claim | Audience | Promise | Mechanism | Proof | CTA | Tone | Weakness | Opportunity |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | AI analytics | Revenue teams | Forecast faster | AI dashboards | logos | demo | technical | vague outcome | own decision workflow |
| B | BI automation | Ops teams | Save time | integrations | metrics | trial | friendly | commodity | focus on trusted numbers |

### Questions to answer

- What language does everyone use?
- What claims sound interchangeable?
- What proof does everyone show?
- What do customers complain about in reviews?
- What alternative is rarely addressed?
- What emotional concern is ignored?
- What buyer segment is underserved?
- What mechanism could be named more clearly?
- What guarantee or pilot could reduce risk?

### Differentiation from competitor sameness

If competitors all say:

> Automate your workflows with AI.

You might say:

> Automate the three approval handoffs that slow every client project: brief signoff, revision decisions, and final delivery approval.

If competitors all say:

> All-in-one dashboard.

You might say:

> A decision dashboard that shows which number changed, who owns it, and what action is due next.

## Category and frame strategy

The category tells buyers how to evaluate you. If you choose the wrong category, you inherit the wrong comparison set.

### Category questions

- What category do buyers already search for?
- What category do buyers already have budget for?
- What category creates unfair comparison?
- What category makes the value obvious?
- What category makes the product sound like a commodity?
- Can the product create a new subcategory?

### Category examples

Product: tool that analyzes customer calls for churn risk.

Possible categories:

- Conversation intelligence.
- Customer success software.
- Churn prediction platform.
- Renewal risk management.
- Customer health analytics.

Different frames create different copy:

Conversation intelligence:

> Search, summarize, and analyze every customer conversation.

Renewal risk management:

> Find the call moments that put renewals at risk before the account health score drops.

Customer success software:

> Give CSMs a clearer account plan from every customer conversation.

Choose based on buyer intent, differentiation, and business strategy.

## Positioning development

### Positioning ingredients

1. Competitive alternatives.
2. Unique capabilities.
3. Value those capabilities enable.
4. Best-fit customers.
5. Market category.
6. Relevant trends.
7. Proof.

### Positioning worksheet

```markdown
## Competitive alternatives
What would customers use if this did not exist?

## Unique capabilities
What can we do that alternatives cannot or do not?

## Value
What valuable outcomes do those capabilities enable?

## Best-fit customers
Who cares most about that value?

## Category
What context helps them understand and value us?

## Trend / why now
What change makes this more urgent now?

## Proof
Why should they believe us?
```

### Positioning example

Product: AI documentation assistant for engineering teams.

Competitive alternatives:

- Static wiki.
- Asking senior engineers.
- Outdated onboarding docs.
- Recording meetings.

Unique capabilities:

- Converts pull requests, design docs, and incident reviews into searchable explanations.
- Flags stale docs.
- Answers with source links.

Value:

- New engineers find answers faster.
- Senior engineers answer fewer repeated questions.
- Technical decisions become easier to audit.

Best-fit customers:

- Engineering orgs with 30+ engineers, rapid onboarding, and frequent architecture changes.

Category:

- Engineering knowledge system.

Positioning statement:

> For engineering teams whose architecture changes faster than their docs, AtlasDocs is an engineering knowledge system that turns pull requests, design docs, and incident reviews into searchable, source-linked answers, unlike static wikis that go stale the moment the next sprint starts.

## Message pillars

A message pillar is a durable argument repeated across assets.

### Pillar template

```markdown
## Pillar name
The big idea.

## Buyer belief to shift
From: ...
To: ...

## Core claim
...

## Why it matters
...

## Proof
...

## Sample copy
...
```

### Example pillars

#### Pillar 1: Decisions, not dashboards

Buyer belief shift:

> From “We need better reports” to “We need every report connected to the decision it should trigger.”

Core claim:

> A dashboard is only useful when it tells the team what to do next.

Sample copy:

> Stop building dashboards that end in “interesting.” Build reports that name the owner, the change, and the next decision.

#### Pillar 2: Adoption beats automation

Buyer belief shift:

> From “More automation will fix this” to “Automation only works when the team trusts the workflow.”

Core claim:

> The best workflow is the one your team actually uses under pressure.

Sample copy:

> Automations fail when they ask busy people to change everything at once. We start with the one handoff your team already hates.

#### Pillar 3: Proof before scale

Buyer belief shift:

> From “We need a platform rollout” to “We need a scoped proof point.”

Core claim:

> Start with one workflow, prove value, then scale.

Sample copy:

> Run one pilot, measure the leak, and expand only when the business case is visible.

## Brand strategy foundations

Brand is not a logo or vibe. In marketing copy, brand is the set of mental associations buyers remember and use when choosing.

### Brand copy should build

- Category memory: what kind of thing this is.
- Distinctive assets: words, phrases, visuals, characters, names, rituals, sounds, formats.
- Emotional associations: calm, confidence, energy, status, safety, ambition.
- Functional associations: speed, reliability, clarity, taste, expertise, convenience.
- Situational memory: when to think of the brand.

### Distinctive asset inventory

```markdown
## Brand name
...

## Tagline / repeated phrase
...

## Signature product names
...

## Signature framework names
...

## Visual motifs
...

## Verbal motifs
...

## Enemy / old way
...

## Rituals / recurring formats
...

## Proof motifs
...
```

Example:

- Repeated phrase: “One calm place for client work.”
- Framework: “The 3-Approval Map.”
- Enemy: scattered approvals.
- Ritual: weekly “approval debt” review.
- Proof motif: before/after project timelines.

## Strategic narrative

A strategic narrative explains what changed, why old approaches are breaking, and why the product represents the new way.

### Strategic narrative structure

1. The world changed.
2. The old way no longer works.
3. This creates a new risk or opportunity.
4. The winning teams adopt a new way.
5. The product enables that new way.
6. The buyer can start with a safe next step.

Example:

> Remote work did not just move meetings online. It scattered decisions across calls, chats, docs, and project boards. That means client work no longer fails because teams lack effort. It fails because approvals disappear. The agencies protecting margins now treat approvals as a workflow, not a memory test. ApprovalDesk gives every client decision a home, an owner, and a timestamp, so projects keep moving without another status meeting.

## Research-led copy angles

Generate angles from research, not brainstorming alone.

### Angle categories

- Pain angle: current suffering.
- Cost angle: measurable loss.
- Speed angle: faster path.
- Ease angle: less effort.
- Risk angle: avoid downside.
- Status angle: look credible, modern, prepared.
- Control angle: regain visibility.
- Identity angle: become the kind of person/team you want to be.
- Contrarian angle: common advice is wrong.
- Mechanism angle: new way works differently.
- Proof angle: evidence leads.
- Comparison angle: unlike alternatives.
- Timing angle: why now.
- Enemy angle: old way causes the pain.

### Angle generation example

Product: onboarding knowledge hub for remote teams.

Pain:

> New hires should not have to ask the same question in three Slack channels.

Cost:

> Every repeated onboarding question is a small tax on your most experienced people.

Speed:

> Help new hires find their first-week answers before their manager wakes up.

Risk:

> Tribal knowledge becomes a hiring risk when your team grows faster than your documentation.

Identity:

> Build the kind of remote team where people can find answers without performing confusion in public.

Contrarian:

> Your onboarding docs are not too short. They are too hard to retrieve at the moment of need.

Mechanism:

> Map answers to the moment new hires need them, not the folder someone guessed they might browse.

## Research-to-copy pipeline

Use this pipeline for major campaigns.

1. Gather raw material.
2. Tag VOC.
3. Identify repeated pains, desired outcomes, objections, and buying criteria.
4. Map segments and awareness stages.
5. Identify trigger events.
6. Build positioning statement.
7. Build message pillars.
8. Create angle bank.
9. Build proof inventory.
10. Draft assets.
11. QA against research.
12. Test and learn.

## When research is limited

Sometimes the user has little data. Do not stop. Use assumptions, label them, and create a research plan.

### Limited-data approach

1. State assumptions.
2. Draft based on likely buyer psychology.
3. Identify highest-risk assumptions.
4. Suggest fast research to validate.
5. Provide copy variants to test.

Example:

> Assumption: the buyer already knows generic project management tools but struggles with client approval chaos. Highest-risk assumption: approvals are more painful than deadlines. Fast validation: review five lost deals, interview three customers, and search support tickets for “approval,” “revision,” and “waiting on client.”

## Fast research prompts for the user

When the user can provide more context, ask:

1. What do customers say right before they buy?
2. What do customers say when they cancel or do not buy?
3. What is the most common failed alternative?
4. What result can you prove today?
5. What claim would your legal/compliance team reject?
6. Who is the buyer afraid of disappointing?
7. What is the most expensive part of doing nothing?
8. What phrase do customers use that your website does not?
9. Which competitor do prospects mention most?
10. Why do your best customers choose you even when you are not the cheapest?

## Research-driven examples

### Example 1: SaaS homepage from VOC

Raw research:

- Buyers say: “Our reports are stale by the time we meet.”
- Failed alternative: spreadsheets and BI dashboards.
- Desired outcome: make decisions during weekly planning.
- Objection: another dashboard will not change behavior.

Messaging:

> **Turn weekly reports into weekly decisions**
>
> PlanBoard connects every metric to an owner, a change, and a next step, so your team stops debating stale dashboards and starts acting before the week is over.
>
> [See the decision workflow]

Why it works:

- Uses customer language: stale reports.
- Reframes category: not another dashboard, a decision workflow.
- Names action: owner, change, next step.

### Example 2: Ecommerce product from reviews

Raw review language:

- “I hate when my shoes touch clean clothes.”
- “Fits under the seat.”
- “I can pack for two nights without checking a bag.”

Product page hero:

> **The under-seat weekender that keeps shoes away from clean clothes**
>
> Pack two nights of outfits, sneakers, toiletries, and a laptop in one carry-on bag with a separate lower compartment for the messy stuff.
>
> [Choose your color]

Why it works:

- Leads with a vivid annoyance.
- Includes use case and constraint.
- Turns a feature into a practical benefit.

### Example 3: Course offer from interviews

Raw research:

- Audience: consultants who know LinkedIn matters but hate posting vague advice.
- Pain: inconsistent content, no point of view.
- Desired outcome: attract better calls without becoming a full-time creator.
- Objection: “I do not want to sound like everyone else.”

Offer messaging:

> **Build a LinkedIn point of view that attracts consulting leads without turning you into a content hamster**
>
> In four weeks, turn your client stories, objections, and frameworks into a repeatable posting system that sounds like you and sells your expertise before the call.

Why it works:

- Names identity resistance.
- Reframes posting as point-of-view building.
- Connects content to sales conversations.

## Strategic diagnosis checklist

Before drafting, answer:

- Is the audience specific enough?
- Is the problem urgent enough?
- Is the desired outcome valuable enough?
- Is the category clear enough?
- Is the differentiator meaningful enough?
- Is the proof strong enough?
- Is the offer easy enough to say yes to?
- Is the next step appropriate for buying stage?
- Is the language grounded in customer reality?
- Is the copy honest about constraints?

## Final rule

Research is not a pre-writing chore. It is the source of the copy. If a line does not connect to a buyer truth, a proof point, an offer component, or a strategic choice, it probably does not belong.
