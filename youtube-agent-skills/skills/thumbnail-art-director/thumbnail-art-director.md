# Purpose

Turn the packaging hypothesis into a compelling visual decision. For faceless channels, replace dependence on reaction faces with objects, outcomes, contrast, scale, evidence, transformation, and recognizable brand grammar.

## Use this skill when

- A packaging brief requires thumbnail concepts or asset capture.
- An existing thumbnail needs a new hypothesis.
- A channel needs a recognizable but flexible visual system.
- Thumbnail production is becoming slow, cluttered, or derivative.

## Required inputs

- Locked packaging brief and inferred viewer promise.
- Available or creatable visual proof.
- Channel visual system and competitor audit.
- Rights-cleared source assets or capture plan.
- Mobile display constraints and test plan.

## Non-negotiable operating rules

- One thumbnail equals one dominant visual idea.
- Use the largest, clearest proof of the promise; decoration is secondary.
- Text is optional and should add information the image cannot efficiently carry.
- Do not fabricate documentary evidence, results, scale, products, people, or events.
- Do not imitate a living creator’s signature style so closely that authorship becomes misleading.

# Workflow

1. Translate each packaging hypothesis into a visual sentence: subject + contrast/action + consequence.
2. Generate 8-15 rough compositions before polishing. Explore object, before/after, scale, versus, anomaly, map, result, and evidence-led routes.
3. Select a single focal point and remove elements that do not improve comprehension, emotion, or curiosity.
4. Plan original capture whenever a custom asset can create credibility or differentiation. For faceless channels, capture hands, environments, screens, artifacts, prototypes, documents, charts, or controlled scenes.
5. Design hierarchy for small display: dominant subject, readable contrast, clean silhouette, sufficient separation, and limited fine detail.
6. Check title complementarity. Remove thumbnail text that merely repeats the title.
7. Verify rights and synthetic-media implications for every asset. Label mockups internally and avoid presenting them as actual results.
8. Create three production-ready variants aligned with the packaging hypotheses, not three color treatments.
9. Run grayscale, blur, and tiny-size tests; then test with cold reviewers.
10. Export at current YouTube-recommended resolution and preserve editable source files and asset provenance.

## Decision rules

- Use a face only when the identity or emotion materially carries the promise; faceless does not require banning human figures.
- Subtle, honest emotion can outperform exaggerated expressions when it matches the channel and story.
- If a thumbnail requires a paragraph to explain, rebuild the concept around stronger visual proof.
- Use brand consistency as recognition, not as a template that prevents adaptation.
- A visual that attracts the wrong audience is a failed thumbnail even if CTR rises.

# Required outputs

- Thumbnail creative brief and visual sentence.
- Rough composition board and selected variants.
- Asset capture/generation list with rights basis.
- Production files, exports, and accessibility description.
- Thumbnail-specific A/B hypothesis notes.

# Quality gate

PASS only when:

- One focal idea survives a tiny-size test.
- The image adds information or emotion beyond the title.
- Every visible claim is true or clearly illustrative.
- Assets are original or rights-cleared with provenance.
- Variants test different viewer interpretations.

# Handoff

Deliver exports and source records to `packaging-director` and `publishing-seo-distribution`; deliver new capture requirements to `faceless-visual-producer`; send rights or synthetic-evidence concerns to `rights-policy-brand-safety`.

## Metrics owned

- CTR and watch-time share by variant.
- One-second comprehension accuracy.
- Thumbnail production time.
- Reuse of original asset systems without template fatigue.
- Frequency of post-publish package revisions.

## Failure modes to prevent

- Clutter, tiny labels, and multiple competing focal points.
- Generic stock collages with no proof.
- Creating misleading scale or fake outcomes.
- Overusing faces because successful creators do.
- Polishing one idea before exploring alternatives.
