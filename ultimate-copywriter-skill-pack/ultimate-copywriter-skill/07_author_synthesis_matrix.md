# 07 — Author and Practitioner Synthesis Matrix

This module turns the classic and contemporary copywriting/marketing canon into operational rules for an agent. It is not a summary of copyrighted books. It is an original synthesis of widely known principles, reframed as practical behaviors, diagnostics, and examples.

Use this file when you want the agent to think through a problem from multiple schools: direct response, brand advertising, positioning, marketing science, behavioral science, content, growth, and modern conversion copywriting.

## How to use this matrix

For any assignment, ask:

1. What would the scientific direct-response tradition test?
2. What would the market-awareness tradition diagnose?
3. What would the positioning school clarify?
4. What would the brand advertising tradition make simpler or more memorable?
5. What would behavioral science warn us about?
6. What would content/growth practitioners turn into a repeatable system?
7. What would conversion copywriters mine from customer language?
8. What would ethical QA reject?

## Direct-response and classic copywriting principles

### Claude Hopkins — specificity, testing, reason-why, samples, demonstration

Operational rule:

> Treat every claim as a testable hypothesis and every benefit as something to demonstrate.

Use when:

- The copy is vague.
- The offer lacks proof.
- The product has a concrete demonstration.
- The user wants direct-response clarity.

Agent behavior:

- Ask what can be measured.
- Replace adjectives with facts.
- Show the mechanism.
- Prefer a concrete reason to believe over style.

Weak:

> Our tool improves productivity.

Hopkins-style improvement:

> See which approval handoffs delayed your last three projects, then fix the one costing the most hours this week.

Sample demonstration copy:

> Upload one project. In under 10 minutes, ApprovalDesk shows every approval stage, stakeholder, missing decision, and next step.

### John E. Kennedy — salesmanship in print and reason why

Operational rule:

> Copy must make a sales argument, not merely announce a product.

Use when:

- The asset sounds like a brochure.
- The headline is clever but unpersuasive.
- The offer needs a clear reason to act.

Agent behavior:

- Build the argument as if speaking to one buyer.
- Include the reason why the product works.
- Make the next step feel logical.

Before:

> Introducing our new client portal.

Reason-why version:

> Give every client one place to approve files because decisions scattered across email and Slack are what create revision loops.

### Robert Collier — enter the conversation already happening in the reader's mind

Operational rule:

> Start with the buyer's active thought, worry, desire, or frustration.

Use when:

- The copy starts from the company's excitement.
- The first line feels generic.
- The audience has strong existing pain.

Agent behavior:

- Write openings from buyer self-talk.
- Use VOC language.
- Lead with the situation, not the product.

Company-centered:

> We are excited to launch our new onboarding platform.

Reader-centered:

> Your new hire knows the answer exists somewhere. They just cannot find it without interrupting the person who is busiest.

### Victor Schwab — headline utility and emotional payoff

Operational rule:

> A headline must promise a reader-relevant reward, not merely label the topic.

Use when:

- The user asks for headline options.
- Existing headlines feel flat.
- You need variants by appeal.

Agent behavior:

- Generate headlines by desire, fear, curiosity, proof, ease, and identity.
- Make each headline earn the next line.

Flat:

> Email Marketing Guide.

Schwab-style alternatives:

> Write the follow-up email that gives buyers a useful reason to reply.
>
> The 4 buyer doubts your nurture sequence should answer before sales calls.
>
> Stop sending every lead the same proof.

### John Caples — tested headlines, self-interest, news, curiosity

Operational rule:

> Headlines should be clear, benefit-led, and testable. Curiosity should serve self-interest.

Use when:

- Paid traffic performance matters.
- Multiple headline variants are needed.
- The copy needs a stronger opening.

Agent behavior:

- Produce direct, news, curiosity, how-to, and proof variants.
- Avoid cleverness that hides the benefit.

Headline set:

Direct:

> Reduce repeated onboarding questions by mapping answers to each new hire's first 30 days.

News:

> New: role-based answer paths for remote onboarding.

Curiosity:

> The onboarding metric that shows whether new hires are confident or dependent.

How-to:

> How to help new hires find answers without interrupting senior teammates.

Proof:

> How one team cut repeated onboarding questions by 31% in six weeks.

### Rosser Reeves — unique selling proposition

Operational rule:

> Find the specific proposition the buyer should remember and competitors cannot easily own.

Use when:

- The copy sounds interchangeable.
- Product positioning is unclear.
- Ads need one memorable claim.

Agent behavior:

- Define one core promise.
- Ensure it is valuable, specific, and differentiating.
- Repeat it consistently.

Weak:

> Better project collaboration.

USP direction:

> The approval workflow that proves who approved which version before a revision loop starts.

### David Ogilvy — research, clarity, long copy when needed, credibility, brand respect

Operational rule:

> Respect the reader's intelligence. Use research, specificity, and complete information when the decision requires it.

Use when:

- The offer is high-consideration.
- The brand needs trust.
- The product has meaningful detail.

Agent behavior:

- Do not assume shorter is always better.
- Include useful facts.
- Avoid empty slogans.
- Make copy readable and credible.

Short but under-informative:

> Smarter analytics for finance teams.

Ogilvy-influenced detail:

> Finance teams use CloseView to reconcile invoice exceptions before month-end close. Each exception shows source, owner, status, and audit trail, so controllers can review the number before it reaches the board report.

### Eugene Schwartz — awareness, sophistication, mass desire, mechanism

Operational rule:

> Match the message to buyer awareness and market sophistication; do not lead with the product when the buyer is not ready for it.

Use when:

- The market is saturated.
- Claims feel familiar.
- Long-form sales pages or VSLs are needed.

Agent behavior:

- Identify awareness stage.
- Identify sophistication level.
- Choose promise/mechanism/belief accordingly.

Example for same product: sales call scoring.

Unaware:

> Your pipeline review may be too late to catch the coaching moments that cost deals.

Problem-aware:

> Managers cannot listen to every discovery call, so deal-stalling patterns go unseen.

Solution-aware:

> Call scoring helps managers find repeated coaching gaps without reviewing every recording manually.

Product-aware:

> DealSignal scores calls against your actual sales playbook and shows missed next steps, weak discovery, and unresolved objections by rep.

Most-aware:

> Start a 30-day call-scoring pilot and review your first manager coaching dashboard this week.

### Gary Halbert — list, offer, personal tone, directness, curiosity with payoff

Operational rule:

> The right audience and a strong offer beat clever copy. Write like one person to one person.

Use when:

- Email feels corporate.
- The offer is unclear.
- The audience needs tighter targeting.

Agent behavior:

- Diagnose list/audience quality.
- Strengthen offer before polishing.
- Use a conversational sales letter tone when appropriate.

Corporate:

> Our organization provides comprehensive solutions for customer success optimization.

Personal/direct:

> If your CSMs manage 100+ renewals a quarter, the risk is not that they do not care. It is that important signals are buried in calls they do not have time to review.

### Gary Bencivenga — proof, believability, credibility, valuable advertising

Operational rule:

> The more valuable and believable the message, the more persuasive it becomes.

Use when:

- Claims are strong but skepticism is high.
- The user needs direct-response credibility.
- The asset lacks proof.

Agent behavior:

- Add proof mechanisms early.
- Offer useful information within the promotion.
- Strengthen credibility before urgency.

Weak:

> Our method works for consultants.

Proof-rich:

> The method starts with the material consultants already have: client stories, repeated objections, and frameworks used in paid work. In week one, you extract 20 post ideas from real sales conversations instead of inventing topics from scratch.

### Joseph Sugarman — slippery slide, seeds of curiosity, product demonstration

Operational rule:

> Every sentence should make the next sentence easier to read.

Use when:

- Long copy feels heavy.
- Product demos need narrative.
- Curiosity can pull the reader through.

Agent behavior:

- Use short opening sentences.
- Create open loops.
- Reveal product value progressively.
- Keep momentum.

Example opening:

> The first signal was not in the dashboard.
>
> It was in a customer call three weeks earlier.
>
> The customer mentioned an integration blocker twice. No one escalated it. The health score stayed green.
>
> Then the renewal slipped.

### Dan Kennedy — direct offer, urgency, no-BS specificity, response orientation

Operational rule:

> Make the offer, deadline, risk reversal, and response path unmistakable.

Use when:

- Copy is too soft.
- The user needs lead generation or sales response.
- The campaign needs clear action.

Agent behavior:

- Add deadlines only when real.
- Clarify guarantee.
- Use response-driven CTAs.
- Write to the economic buyer.

Soft:

> Reach out if you would like to learn more.

Direct:

> Book a 20-minute audit by Friday and we will review your homepage, CTA, and proof placement before the call.

### Jay Abraham — strategy of preeminence, leverage, risk reversal, lifetime value

Operational rule:

> Become the buyer's trusted advisor by helping them make a better decision, even before they buy.

Use when:

- The brand needs trust.
- The offer can be improved through risk reversal.
- The business has multiple growth levers.

Agent behavior:

- Add education, diagnostics, and decision support.
- Identify upsell, cross-sell, referral, retention opportunities.
- Improve perceived value and reduce risk.

Example:

> Not sure whether the sprint is the right next step? Apply anyway. If your homepage problem is really positioning, proof, or traffic quality, we will tell you before recommending a rewrite.

### Bob Bly — practical B2B clarity and complete copy

Operational rule:

> Explain the product clearly enough for a rational buyer to understand value, features, proof, and next step.

Use when:

- B2B copy is vague.
- Technical products need plain explanation.
- The user needs brochures, white papers, landing pages, or lead gen.

Agent behavior:

- Define terms.
- State benefits plainly.
- Include enough detail to support inquiry.

Example:

> The platform imports invoice data from your ERP, flags mismatches by vendor and amount, assigns each exception to an owner, and keeps an audit trail for month-end close.

### Drayton Bird — direct marketing common sense, relevance, testing, continuity

Operational rule:

> Write for response, track the response, and keep communicating after the first action.

Use when:

- User needs lifecycle marketing.
- Campaign must be measurable.
- Copy must be practical.

Agent behavior:

- Define measurable objective.
- Align follow-up sequence.
- Consider retention and repeat purchase.

Example:

> The landing page gets the booking. The confirmation page sets expectations. The pre-call email collects context. The follow-up email advances the decision. Do not judge the page alone.

### Lester Wunderman — direct relationship and database-driven relevance

Operational rule:

> Use known customer context to make communication more relevant and useful.

Use when:

- Email lifecycle, CRM, segmentation, retention.
- Personalized journeys.

Agent behavior:

- Segment by behavior and stage.
- Trigger messages based on user actions.
- Avoid fake personalization.

Example:

> Since you downloaded the onboarding checklist but have not mapped your first role path, here is the 5-step version most teams use to start.

### Brian Kurtz — overdeliver, list trust, direct-response legacy

Operational rule:

> Treat the audience as an asset. Deliver value before, during, and after the sale.

Use when:

- User wants email, newsletter, community, or long-term list monetization.

Agent behavior:

- Avoid burning trust for short-term clicks.
- Add useful education to promotions.
- Maintain cadence and relevance.

Example:

> Even if you do not join the sprint, use this simple test: move your first proof point directly under the biggest claim on your homepage.

## Brand advertising and creative principles

### Bill Bernbach — simplicity, truth, human insight, idea over formula

Operational rule:

> Find the human truth and express it simply.

Use when:

- Copy is formulaic.
- Brand needs memorability.
- Product has a simple emotional truth.

Agent behavior:

- Cut clutter.
- Find contrast.
- Say the true thing plainly.

Example:

> The approval exists. No one can find it.

### Leo Burnett — inherent drama and brand characters/assets

Operational rule:

> Find the inherent drama inside the product or situation.

Use when:

- Product seems ordinary.
- Brand needs distinctive assets.
- Ecommerce or consumer copy needs sensory life.

Agent behavior:

- Identify the product's most vivid use moment.
- Build brand assets around recurring symbols, phrases, or rituals.

Example:

> The lower compartment is not a feature. It is the moment your muddy shoes do not touch the clean shirt you packed for dinner.

### Howard Gossage — conversation, participation, wit with purpose

Operational rule:

> Advertising should invite response, not merely broadcast.

Use when:

- Brand wants engagement.
- Social or community copy.
- Campaigns with participation.

Agent behavior:

- Ask sharp questions.
- Make the reader part of the idea.
- Avoid empty noise.

Example:

> Open your project board. Can you prove which version the client approved? If not, the task is tracked but the decision is not.

### James Webb Young — idea generation through combination

Operational rule:

> New ideas often come from combining old elements in new ways.

Use when:

- Need campaign concepts.
- Need angles from research.

Agent behavior:

- Gather raw material.
- Combine buyer quote + mechanism + proof + cultural context.
- Incubate and create multiple angles.

Example combinations:

- Buyer quote: “the approval is somewhere.”
- Mechanism: decision record.
- Cultural context: remote work scattered decisions.
- Campaign idea: “Somewhere is not a workflow.”

### David Abbott — elegance, empathy, long-copy grace

Operational rule:

> Treat readers with warmth, intelligence, and restraint.

Use when:

- Premium brand or thoughtful service.
- Long-form brand copy.
- Tone must be human.

Agent behavior:

- Write clean, elegant prose.
- Avoid shouting.
- Use story and specificity.

Example:

> You do not notice a good approval trail when everything is going well. You notice it on the day the client asks why the final file changed. That is when a clear record stops being admin and starts protecting the relationship.

### Neil French — long copy, voice, narrative seduction

Operational rule:

> Long copy can work when the voice rewards attention and the product deserves explanation.

Use when:

- Premium, complex, or story-rich offers.
- Print-style or brand storytelling.

Agent behavior:

- Build rhythm.
- Use vivid details.
- Let copy sell through personality and argument.

Example opening:

> There is a particular kind of silence that follows a client asking, “Did we approve this?” Everyone remembers the meeting. Nobody remembers the version. That silence is expensive.

### Dan Wieden / Lee Clow school — cultural simplicity and brand belief

Operational rule:

> A brand phrase can become powerful when it captures a belief the audience wants to inhabit.

Use when:

- Tagline, manifesto, campaign idea.
- Brand wants emotional memory.

Agent behavior:

- Identify the buyer's aspirational identity.
- Strip the line down.
- Avoid over-explaining.

Examples:

> Decisions deserve a home.
>
> Coach the pattern.
>
> Findability beats more docs.
>
> Reports should end in decisions.

### Dave Trott — predatory thinking, simplicity, lateral problem-solving

Operational rule:

> Reframe the problem before solving it.

Use when:

- User is stuck on tactics.
- Competitive sameness is high.

Agent behavior:

- Ask what everyone else assumes.
- Attack the hidden bottleneck.
- Write sharply.

Example:

> Everyone is trying to make project boards better. But the project board was not built to prove client decisions.

### Luke Sullivan — concept, campaign craft, idea discipline

Operational rule:

> Build campaigns around one clear creative idea that can stretch across executions.

Use when:

- User needs campaign platform.
- Multiple ads/assets needed.

Agent behavior:

- Create the big idea.
- Show executions across channels.
- Keep the idea consistent.

Campaign idea:

> “Somewhere is not a workflow.”

Executions:

- Ad: “The approval is somewhere. That is the problem.”
- Landing page: “Turn scattered approvals into one decision record.”
- Email: “Can your team prove what the client approved?”
- Social: screenshot-style mock “somewhere in Slack” thread.

## Positioning and marketing strategy principles

### Philip Kotler — segmentation, targeting, positioning, marketing mix

Operational rule:

> Define the market, choose the target, position the offer, and align product, price, place, and promotion.

Use when:

- User asks for strategy.
- Audience is too broad.
- Marketing mix is inconsistent.

Agent behavior:

- Segment by meaningful buying variables.
- Pick primary target.
- Align offer and channel.

Example:

> Do not market this as “for all remote teams.” Target engineering teams onboarding 10+ developers a quarter, position it as an engineering knowledge system, price by team size, and promote through onboarding pain and senior-engineer interruption cost.

### Theodore Levitt — marketing myopia and customer need

Operational rule:

> Do not define the business by the product; define it by the customer progress it enables.

Use when:

- Copy overfocuses on features.
- The company sells a commodity.

Agent behavior:

- Ask what job the buyer hires the product for.
- Reframe around outcome.

Product-defined:

> We sell dashboards.

Need-defined:

> We help teams make the weekly decisions that dashboards are supposed to inform.

### Ries and Trout — positioning in the mind, category, focus

Operational rule:

> Own a clear position in the buyer's mind by choosing a simple frame and avoiding message sprawl.

Use when:

- Brand has many messages.
- Competitive category is crowded.

Agent behavior:

- Pick one primary category frame.
- Sharpen contrast with alternatives.
- Repeat the position.

Example:

> Not a project management tool. A client approval workflow.

### Geoffrey Moore — crossing the chasm, beachhead market

Operational rule:

> For new or complex products, win a specific beachhead segment with a complete solution before broad expansion.

Use when:

- Product is early-stage.
- Audience is too broad.
- Tech category is emerging.

Agent behavior:

- Identify the niche with urgent pain.
- Write for pragmatists with proof and implementation.

Example:

> Start with cybersecurity sales teams that already record calls and need to coach complex discovery, not every sales team in every industry.

### Clayton Christensen — jobs to be done and progress

Operational rule:

> Understand the progress the buyer seeks in a specific circumstance.

Use when:

- User asks for positioning or messaging.
- Product competes with unexpected alternatives.

Agent behavior:

- Use “When..., I want..., so I can...”
- Identify functional, emotional, and social jobs.

Example:

> When I have to convince finance to fund a workflow tool, I want a one-slide business case, so I can avoid a vague software discussion and get a pilot approved.

### David Aaker / Kevin Keller — brand equity and associations

Operational rule:

> Build consistent, valuable associations around the brand.

Use when:

- Brand guide, messaging system, long-term strategy.

Agent behavior:

- Identify desired associations.
- Repeat key phrases and proof motifs.
- Protect consistency.

Example associations:

- ApprovalDesk = client decisions, proof, margin protection, calm delivery.
- OnboardBase = findability, new-hire confidence, fewer interruptions.

### Byron Sharp / Jenni Romaniuk — mental availability and distinctive assets

Operational rule:

> Make the brand easy to notice, remember, and buy in relevant situations.

Use when:

- Brand wants growth beyond direct-response campaigns.
- Messaging needs distinctiveness.

Agent behavior:

- Build category entry points.
- Use consistent distinctive assets.
- Avoid over-targeting so narrowly that memory never builds.

Category entry point examples:

- “When a client asks who approved the final file.”
- “When a new hire asks the same question again.”
- “When the dashboard is ready but the decision is not.”

Distinctive verbal assets:

- “Decisions deserve a home.”
- “Findability beats more docs.”
- “Coach the pattern, not the anecdote.”

### Binet and Field — balance long-term brand and short-term activation

Operational rule:

> Use brand activity to build future demand and activation to convert current demand.

Use when:

- User overfocuses on performance or only brand.
- Campaign planning.

Agent behavior:

- Separate memory-building from conversion assets.
- Keep brand idea consistent while changing CTAs by funnel stage.

Example:

Brand line:

> Decisions deserve a home.

Activation landing page:

> Start a 14-day approval workflow pilot on one live project.

### Mark Ritson — diagnosis before strategy before tactics

Operational rule:

> Do not jump to channels before market diagnosis and strategic choices.

Use when:

- User asks for social posts without strategy.
- User asks for tactics disconnected from objective.

Agent behavior:

- Diagnose audience, objective, positioning, and budget/context.
- Then recommend tactics.

Example:

> Before choosing LinkedIn ads, define whether the goal is category education, demo capture, retargeting, or sales enablement. The copy changes for each.

### April Dunford — positioning as context-setting

Operational rule:

> Help buyers understand why the product matters by choosing the right competitive frame and value themes.

Use when:

- Innovative product is misunderstood.
- Buyers compare against wrong alternatives.

Agent behavior:

- Identify alternatives.
- Identify unique capabilities and value.
- Choose category.
- Translate to sales narrative.

Example:

> If buyers compare you to project management, they ask about tasks. If they compare you to approval workflow, they ask about decision records. Choose the frame that makes the value obvious.

### Seth Godin — permission, remarkable, smallest viable audience, worldview

Operational rule:

> Earn attention from a specific audience by being useful, distinct, and aligned with what they care about.

Use when:

- Content strategy.
- Community building.
- Creator/brand voice.

Agent behavior:

- Define smallest viable audience.
- Create opt-in value.
- Avoid interruption without relevance.

Example:

> This newsletter is for consultants who want better inbound conversations without becoming full-time creators. Every issue turns one client story into a post idea.

## Behavioral science and persuasion principles

### Aristotle — ethos, pathos, logos

Operational rule:

> Balance credibility, emotion, and logic.

Use when:

- Copy is too rational or too emotional.

Agent behavior:

- Ethos: why trust the source.
- Pathos: why care.
- Logos: why it makes sense.

Example:

> Pathos: stop worrying about hidden renewal risks.
> Logos: signals are detected from call patterns.
> Ethos: teams managing 100+ renewals use it in weekly account reviews.

### Robert Cialdini — influence principles used ethically

Operational rule:

> Use influence principles to reduce decision friction, not to deceive.

Principles and ethical uses:

- Reciprocity: give useful audits, checklists, examples.
- Commitment: start with small diagnostic action.
- Social proof: show relevant customer proof.
- Authority: show credentials and expertise honestly.
- Liking: use human voice and shared understanding.
- Scarcity: use only real scarcity.
- Unity: show shared identity when true.

Example:

> Join 300+ CS leaders who use the renewal risk checklist before QBR season.

### Kahneman/Tversky — cognitive ease, loss aversion, framing, heuristics

Operational rule:

> Make good decisions cognitively easier and frame stakes clearly.

Use when:

- Complex offers.
- Risk-sensitive decisions.

Agent behavior:

- Simplify choices.
- Use concrete examples.
- Show cost of inaction honestly.
- Reduce ambiguity.

Example:

> Choose one workflow, one team, and one success metric. You do not need to decide on a company-wide rollout today.

### Thaler/Sunstein — choice architecture and nudges

Operational rule:

> Structure choices so the helpful next step is easy and transparent.

Use when:

- Pricing, onboarding, forms, product UX copy.

Agent behavior:

- Use sensible defaults.
- Clarify options.
- Avoid dark patterns.

Example:

> Recommended: start with the Growth plan if you manage more than 50 customer handoffs a month. You can downgrade before renewal.

### BJ Fogg — behavior model: motivation, ability, prompt

Operational rule:

> A CTA works when motivation, ability, and prompt converge.

Use when:

- CTAs underperform.
- Onboarding fails.

Agent behavior:

- Increase motivation with relevance/value.
- Increase ability by reducing friction.
- Prompt clearly at the right moment.

Example:

> Instead of “Set up your account,” write “Import one live project so your first approval can be captured today.”

### Jonah Berger — triggers, social currency, practical value, emotion, stories

Operational rule:

> Make ideas easier to share by tying them to triggers, usefulness, emotion, and story.

Use when:

- Content and word-of-mouth campaigns.

Agent behavior:

- Attach idea to recurring situation.
- Make the sharer look helpful.
- Package practical value.

Example:

> Trigger: before pipeline review.
> Shareable asset: “The 5 deal-risk questions to ask before the meeting.”

### Chip and Dan Heath — sticky ideas: simple, unexpected, concrete, credible, emotional, stories

Operational rule:

> Make messages simple, surprising, concrete, credible, emotional, and story-driven.

Use when:

- Brand message or campaign idea feels forgettable.

Example:

Simple:

> The task is tracked. The decision is not.

Concrete:

> Version, owner, timestamp, next step.

Story:

> The team had approval but could not prove which file was final.

## Content, inbound, and growth principles

### Ann Handley — useful, clear, human writing

Operational rule:

> Every piece of content should be useful, clear, and written like a human helping another human.

Use when:

- Blog, newsletter, social, educational content.

Agent behavior:

- Cut throat-clearing.
- Add examples.
- Make advice practical.

Example:

> Do not write “be specific.” Show the rewrite: “improve productivity” becomes “cut the three spreadsheet exports before Monday reporting.”

### Joe Pulizzi / Robert Rose — content mission and owned audience

Operational rule:

> Define a content promise that serves an audience consistently enough to build an owned relationship.

Use when:

- Newsletter, blog, media strategy.

Agent behavior:

- Define audience + topic + outcome.
- Avoid random content.

Example content mission:

> Every Tuesday, B2B marketers get one teardown showing how customer language becomes clearer homepage copy.

### David Meerman Scott — timely, buyer-focused, direct publishing

Operational rule:

> Publish what buyers need when they need it, not only polished brand announcements.

Use when:

- Newsjacking, product education, PR/content.

Example:

> When a regulation changes, publish the plain-language checklist buyers need to respond before pitching the product.

### Brian Halligan/Dharmesh Shah — inbound relevance and helpful conversion paths

Operational rule:

> Attract with useful content, convert with relevant offers, nurture with context.

Use when:

- Funnel and lead magnet strategy.

Example:

> Blog post on onboarding checklist → downloadable first-value template → nurture sequence on risk signals → demo CTA for onboarding platform.

### Rand Fishkin — audience research, search intent, discoverability

Operational rule:

> Understand where the audience already searches, asks, compares, and learns.

Use when:

- SEO and content distribution.

Agent behavior:

- Map intent and channels.
- Do not rely on publishing alone.

Example:

> If buyers search “customer onboarding checklist,” serve the checklist first, then explain how software helps operationalize it.

### Avinash Kaushik — analytics, measurement, actionable insight

Operational rule:

> Measure what leads to better decisions, not vanity metrics alone.

Use when:

- Performance analysis and testing.

Agent behavior:

- Identify primary and secondary metrics.
- Tie metrics to business objective.

Example:

> A higher demo conversion rate matters only if demo quality and opportunity creation do not fall.

### Sean Ellis / growth practitioners — experimentation and activation

Operational rule:

> Growth comes from systematic experimentation across acquisition, activation, retention, referral, and revenue.

Use when:

- User asks for growth campaigns.

Agent behavior:

- Build hypotheses.
- Prioritize high-impact tests.
- Track learnings.

Example:

> Test “See sample scorecard” vs. “Book demo” for cold traffic because proof may be needed before commitment.

### Joanna Wiebe / conversion copywriting — customer language, research, message hierarchy

Operational rule:

> Conversion copy should be built from customer research and organized by decision priority.

Use when:

- Landing pages, SaaS pages, email funnels, checkout.

Agent behavior:

- Mine VOC.
- Map anxieties and motivations.
- Write in message hierarchy: most important value first.

Example:

> Customer quote: “I do not want another tool reps ignore.”
>
> Objection block: “No new dashboard for reps to babysit. Coaching cards appear inside the CRM workflow your team already uses.”

### Alex Hormozi — offer strength, value equation, clear promise

Operational rule:

> Increase perceived value by improving outcome, belief, speed, ease, and risk reversal.

Use when:

- Offer feels weak.
- User wants acquisition-focused messaging.

Agent behavior:

- Strengthen offer before words.
- Add guarantees, speed, implementation, bonuses.

Weak:

> Marketing consulting.

Offer-strength version:

> In 21 days, get a researched homepage message, three sales emails, and a 90-day content plan built from customer interviews. If the message map is not clear enough for sales to use, we revise it free.

### Russell Brunson / funnel school — value ladder, hooks, stories, offers

Operational rule:

> Match hook, story, offer, and next step across a funnel.

Use when:

- Webinar, lead magnet, launch, info product, funnel.

Agent behavior:

- Build front-end offer, nurture, core offer, upsell/retention path.
- Use story to create belief.

Example:

> Hook: “Your homepage may not need a redesign.”
> Story: vague homepage became buyer-specific after customer-language audit.
> Offer: Homepage Clarity Sprint.

### Donald Miller / StoryBrand-style clarity — customer as hero, guide, plan, CTA

Operational rule:

> Make the customer the hero. The brand is the guide with a plan.

Use when:

- Homepage clarity.
- Small business/service copy.

Agent behavior:

- Lead with customer problem and desired success.
- Provide a simple plan.
- CTA clearly.

Example:

> You should not have to guess which client approval is final. ApprovalDesk gives your team a simple plan: map the approval stages, invite reviewers, capture every decision.

### Perry Marshall — 80/20 and message leverage

Operational rule:

> Find the few segments, offers, channels, and messages producing most results.

Use when:

- Optimization and prioritization.

Agent behavior:

- Focus on highest-intent pages, biggest objections, best customers, most profitable segments.

Example:

> Rewrite the pricing page FAQ before rewriting 40 blog posts if sales calls show implementation fear is the main blocker.

### April Dunford + Andy Raskin — positioning and strategic narrative for B2B

Operational rule:

> B2B buyers need a clear story about what changed, why old approaches fail, and why the new way matters.

Use when:

- Sales deck, enterprise homepage, category creation.

Example narrative:

> Customer conversations now contain the earliest renewal risks, but most CS systems still depend on lagging health scores. The new way is to turn every conversation into risk signals before the renewal date.

## Practical multi-school diagnostic

When reviewing copy, use these voices:

- Hopkins: Can we prove or demonstrate this?
- Kennedy: Is there a reason why?
- Collier: Does it enter the buyer's current conversation?
- Caples: Does the headline offer self-interest, news, or curiosity?
- Schwartz: Is this matched to awareness and sophistication?
- Ogilvy: Is it credible, researched, and clear?
- Halbert: Is the audience/list and offer strong enough?
- Bencivenga: Is belief strong enough?
- Bernbach: Is there a simple human truth?
- Ries/Trout: Is the position clear in the mind?
- Kotler: Are segment, target, and positioning aligned?
- Christensen: What job is the buyer hiring this for?
- Sharp/Romaniuk: Will this build memory and distinctiveness?
- Cialdini: Are influence principles used ethically?
- Fogg: Is the next behavior easy enough?
- Handley: Is it useful and human?
- Wiebe: Is it grounded in customer language?
- Hormozi: Is the offer strong enough?
- Ritson: Are we doing diagnosis before tactics?

## Example: one product through multiple lenses

Product: onboarding knowledge system.

### Direct-response lens

> Reduce repeated onboarding questions by turning scattered docs into role-based answer paths for each new hire's first 30 days.

### Brand lens

> Findability beats more docs.

### Positioning lens

> Not a wiki. An answer path for new-hire confidence.

### Behavioral lens

> New hires will use the path because it appears at the moment they need the answer, not in a folder they have to browse.

### Content lens

> Publish a guide: “Why onboarding docs fail when teams ignore retrieval.”

### Offer lens

> Start with one role. We map the first 30 days, import existing resources, and identify the 20 questions new hires ask most often.

### Sales enablement lens

> Every repeated question is an interruption tax on senior teammates. The pilot measures repeat questions before and after the answer path launches.

## Final synthesis rule

Do not imitate any author's style exactly. Extract principles. Combine them to fit the buyer, offer, market, channel, and ethical constraints. Great copy is not a museum of formulas; it is a useful decision path written in the language of the buyer.
