# Purpose

Make the channel sound authored and emotionally intentional. Turn the script into natural narration, use sound to guide attention and feeling, and deliver intelligible mixes across phones, headphones, TVs, and captions.

## Use this skill when

- A script is locked for narration.
- The channel uses human or synthetic voiceover.
- Retention suffers from monotone delivery, poor intelligibility, or exhausting sound design.
- Music or voice licensing and disclosure must be managed.
- A sleep video needs stable low-arousal narration, ambience, and mix rules.

## Required inputs

- Locked script with emotional and pronunciation notes.
- Brand voice profile and target viewer.
- Reference mix, music/SFX licenses, and platform delivery specifications.
- AI disclosure and voice-consent requirements.
- Picture timing or animatic where available.

## Non-negotiable operating rules

- Narration must sound like a person communicating an idea, not text being read.
- Never clone or imitate a real person’s voice without authorization.
- Synthetic voice requires licensed commercial use and human editorial control.
- Music and SFX support story turns; they do not compensate for weak writing.
- Speech intelligibility outranks loudness and spectacle.
- Sleep mixes must avoid sudden effects, loud CTAs, music swells, and abrupt volume changes.

# Workflow

1. Create a voice brief: persona, warmth, authority, energy range, humor, pace, pronunciation, and prohibited affectations.
2. Mark the script for emphasis, pauses, breaths, emotional shifts, sentence grouping, and visual synchronization.
3. Record or synthesize short sections so performance can adapt to story beats; capture alternate reads for the opening and key lines.
4. Edit for natural cadence. Remove distracting breaths and errors without flattening all humanity.
5. Select licensed music based on narrative function: anticipation, motion, discovery, tension, relief, reflection, or payoff.
6. Design SFX around meaningful actions, transitions, reveals, and visual proof. Avoid constant whooshes or impact sounds.
7. Build a dynamic mix that lets important scenes breathe and preserves contrast. Duck music under speech and check consonant clarity.
8. Verify names, numbers, units, foreign words, and sponsor pronunciations against the research packet.
9. Mix and review on multiple playback systems. Check captions against the final spoken track.
10. Archive stems, licenses, voice settings, consent records, and final mix notes.

## Decision rules

- Vary pace with meaning; do not read every sentence at maximum speed.
- Use silence when it increases tension, scale, sincerity, or comprehension.
- A human narrator is preferred when personality and trust are core; a synthetic voice is acceptable when licensed, transparent where required, and editorially distinctive.
- Do not place an aggressive music change every few seconds; align changes to story beats.
- If the narration feels generic, rewrite for voice before adding processing.

# Required outputs

- Voice brief and annotated script.
- Final narration takes and alternates.
- Music/SFX cue sheet with licenses.
- Dialogue, music, effects stems and final mix.
- Pronunciation, voice-rights, and AI-use log.

# Quality gate

PASS only when:

- Every word is intelligible on small speakers.
- The opening has intentional performance variation and no wasted lead-in.
- Music and effects follow narrative change rather than habit.
- Voice and music rights are documented.
- Captions match the final audio and important non-speech information is represented.

# Handoff

Deliver stems and mix notes to `retention-video-editor`; send disclosure and licensing records to `rights-policy-brand-safety`; send final audio/caption corrections to `publishing-seo-distribution`.

## Metrics owned

- Audio-related viewer complaints.
- Retention changes after narration or mix improvements.
- Pickup and correction rate.
- Music/SFX licensing compliance.
- Voice production hours per finished minute.

## Failure modes to prevent

- Monotone AI narration over generic visuals.
- Over-compressing and over-limiting until speech is tiring.
- Using unlicensed popular music as a placeholder that reaches final export.
- Mispronouncing names or technical terms.
- Filling every quiet moment with sound.
