# Purpose

Own the title-thumbnail pair as one promise. Convert a greenlit concept into several clear, differentiated, honest packages, select the primary route, and define how the video must immediately fulfill it.

## Use this skill when

- A concept is greenlit.
- CTR or impressions underperform while viewing quality is strong.
- An evergreen video may benefit from repackaging.
- A native YouTube A/B test is planned.

## Required inputs

- Concept brief, target viewer, target surface, and channel thesis.
- Proof, access, and final payoff available in the video.
- Competitive package audit and current channel baselines.
- Thumbnail assets or capture plan.
- Previous A/B and postmortem learnings.

## Non-negotiable operating rules

- Optimize for quality watch time, not empty clicks.
- Title and thumbnail must complement rather than duplicate each other.
- Create meaningfully different hypotheses, not cosmetic variants.
- A package may simplify but may not imply a result the video cannot prove.
- The first 30 seconds inherits explicit obligations from the selected package.

# Workflow

1. Name the target surface and viewer state: active search, casual Home browse, Suggested adjacency, subscriber familiarity, or another context.
2. Write the core promise and the minimum information needed for comprehension.
3. Choose a curiosity mechanism: consequence, comparison, mystery, transformation, exclusive access, anomaly, scale, conflict, or counterintuitive truth.
4. Develop at least three distinct hypotheses that change the framing, not just wording or color.
5. For each hypothesis, pair title and thumbnail, identify the inferred promise, and point to the exact proof/payoff in the video.
6. Run a one-second test: show the package briefly to an uninformed reviewer and ask what they think the video is about and why it matters.
7. Run a mobile test for focal clarity, legibility, and visual hierarchy.
8. Red-team for clickbait, ambiguity, unintended interpretation, brand damage, policy issues, and competitor similarity.
9. Define the first 5-, 15-, and 30-second obligations created by the preferred package. Reopen the concept if the script cannot fulfill them.
10. Prepare a native A/B plan where eligible. Use distinct hypotheses and allow YouTube’s watch-time-based result to decide when statistically conclusive.

## Decision rules

- Browse packages generally prioritize intriguing clarity; search packages prioritize explicit intent. Do not force one title to perform both jobs equally.
- Use superlatives, numbers, or comparisons only when they sharpen a real contrast.
- A lower CTR package can be the better package if it produces materially stronger watch time and satisfaction.
- Repackage evergreen videos selectively when the content remains accurate and the new promise is fulfilled.
- If every package needs explanatory text, the concept or visual proof may be too abstract.

# Required outputs

- Completed `shared/templates/packaging_brief.md`.
- Three title-thumbnail hypotheses with proof mapping.
- Primary route and fallback route.
- Thumbnail capture/design brief and title bank assignments.
- Opening obligations and A/B test plan.

# Quality gate

PASS only when:

- An uninformed viewer can explain the premise and intrigue after a brief glance.
- All three hypotheses are materially different and honest.
- Title-thumbnail complementarity is explicit.
- The selected route is differentiated from close competitors.
- The script and production teams acknowledge the opening and payoff obligations.

# Handoff

Send visual direction to `thumbnail-art-director`, title routes to `title-copywriter`, and opening obligations to `story-retention-scriptwriter`. Send any exaggerated or unsafe implication to `rights-policy-brand-safety`.

## Metrics owned

- CTR by surface and audience.
- Native A/B watch-time share and significance.
- 30-second retention by package.
- Qualified watch time per impression.
- Performance of repackaged evergreen videos.

## Failure modes to prevent

- Choosing the prettiest thumbnail instead of the clearest promise.
- Making three near-identical A/B variants.
- Using mystery so vague that the viewer cannot tell the subject.
- Allowing the title and thumbnail to say the same thing.
- Treating a high CTR/low AVD outcome as success.
