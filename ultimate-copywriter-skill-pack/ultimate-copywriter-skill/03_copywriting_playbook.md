# 03 — Copywriting Playbook: Headlines, Leads, Sales Arguments, Proof, Bullets, CTAs, Email, Ads, Pages, and VSLs

This module is the craft engine. Use it to turn strategy and offer into persuasive words.

## The copywriter's operating sequence

For any asset, move through this sequence:

1. Define the reader and buying stage.
2. Choose one core promise.
3. Choose one core mechanism.
4. Choose one dominant angle.
5. Build the argument spine.
6. Draft the asset.
7. Add proof where skepticism appears.
8. Handle objections.
9. Strengthen the CTA.
10. Edit for clarity, specificity, rhythm, and trust.

## The persuasion spine

A complete sales argument answers these questions:

1. Who is this for?
2. What problem or desire does it address?
3. What result is promised?
4. Why is the old way failing?
5. What is the new mechanism or better way?
6. Why should I believe it?
7. What exactly do I get?
8. Why is this different?
9. What risks are reduced?
10. What should I do next?

### Spine example

Offer: guided onboarding hub for remote SaaS teams.

> Remote SaaS teams with 10+ hires a quarter often do not have an onboarding content problem. They have a retrieval problem: the answer exists, but the new hire cannot find it at the moment they need it. OnboardBase turns scattered docs, Looms, checklists, and Slack answers into role-based answer paths for the first 30 days. New hires find answers without interrupting senior teammates, managers see where people are stuck, and the team can update stale knowledge before it spreads. Start with one role and launch the first path this week.

## Headlines

A headline should make the intended reader think one of these:

- “This is for me.”
- “That is exactly my problem.”
- “I want that outcome.”
- “I have not heard it explained that way.”
- “I need to know how this works.”
- “That proof is worth reading.”
- “This may solve the thing I am dealing with now.”

## Headline scoring checklist

Score 1-5:

- Relevance: Does the intended reader recognize themselves?
- Clarity: Is the meaning instantly understandable?
- Specificity: Does it avoid vague hype?
- Promise: Is the outcome valuable?
- Credibility: Is it believable enough to keep reading?
- Differentiation: Does it avoid sounding like every competitor?
- Momentum: Does it earn the next line?

## Headline formulas with examples

### 1. Outcome without pain

Formula:

> [Achieve desired outcome] without [painful tradeoff].

Examples:

> Book qualified consulting calls without posting generic advice every day.

> Keep client projects moving without chasing approvals across email and Slack.

> Launch your first onboarding path without rewriting every internal doc.

### 2. Specific audience + outcome

Formula:

> [Outcome] for [specific audience].

Examples:

> Renewal risk alerts for customer success teams managing 100+ accounts.

> A weekly content system for consultants who sell expertise, not templates.

> Approval workflows for agencies with too many client revision loops.

### 3. Problem diagnosis

Formula:

> Your [surface problem] is really a [deeper problem].

Examples:

> Your team does not have a documentation problem. It has a findability problem.

> Your funnel is not leaking because leads are lazy. It is leaking because every lead gets the same argument.

> Your dashboard is not broken. The decision workflow around it is.

### 4. Stop doing X

Formula:

> Stop [common behavior] until [important condition].

Examples:

> Stop writing more content until your best ideas have a distribution system.

> Stop sending nurture emails until you know which objection each lead still has.

> Stop buying tools before you map the workflow your team already avoids.

### 5. Mechanism-led

Formula:

> The [mechanism] that helps [audience] [outcome].

Examples:

> The 3-Approval Map that helps agencies protect margins on client work.

> The Renewal Risk Radar that shows CSMs which accounts need attention before the health score drops.

> The Trigger-First Outreach System that gives every cold email a reason to exist.

### 6. Proof-led

Formula:

> How [specific customer] achieved [specific result] by [mechanism].

Examples:

> How a 24-person agency cut revision rounds by 38% with one approval workflow.

> How one SaaS team reduced repeated onboarding questions by 31% in six weeks.

> How a consultant turned 12 client stories into 30 days of lead-generating content.

### 7. Time-bound result

Formula:

> [Outcome] in [timeframe] without [tradeoff].

Examples:

> Build your first buyer-language message map in one afternoon.

> Launch a cleaner onboarding path this week without rebuilding your entire wiki.

> Find your top three funnel leaks in 10 business days.

### 8. Hidden cost

Formula:

> The [hidden cost] of [common behavior].

Examples:

> The hidden payroll cost of answering the same onboarding questions every week.

> The margin leak hiding inside client revision rounds.

> The qualified demos you lose when every follow-up says the same thing.

### 9. The better way

Formula:

> A better way to [job] when [condition].

Examples:

> A better way to coach sales calls when managers cannot review every recording.

> A better way to manage approvals when clients live in email.

> A better way to write content when your best material is trapped in client calls.

### 10. Question headline

Formula:

> What if [desired future or provocative reframing]?

Examples:

> What if every customer call produced a renewal action plan?

> What if your homepage explained the sales conversation your best reps already have?

> What if new hires could find answers before asking your busiest people?

### 11. Contrast headline

Formula:

> Not [old way]. [New way].

Examples:

> Not more reports. Better decisions.

> Not another content calendar. A point of view your buyers remember.

> Not call summaries. Coaching actions managers can use.

### 12. Category reframe

Formula:

> [Product] is not [commodity category]. It is [more valuable category].

Examples:

> This is not a wiki. It is an answer path for every new hire's first 30 days.

> This is not a dashboard. It is a decision system for weekly planning.

> This is not a copy template pack. It is a buyer-language engine for your next campaign.

### 13. Specific pain quote

Formula:

> “I am tired of [VOC phrase].”

Examples:

> Tired of hunting through Slack for the final client approval?

> Tired of rewriting follow-up emails that still do not get replies?

> Tired of dashboards nobody trusts by the time the meeting starts?

### 14. Before/after headline

Formula:

> From [old state] to [new state].

Examples:

> From scattered onboarding docs to one role-based path for every new hire.

> From stale campaign reports to weekly revenue decisions.

> From vague service packages to an offer buyers can understand in one sentence.

### 15. Belief-shift headline

Formula:

> The fastest way to [outcome] is not [common tactic]. It is [better mechanism].

Examples:

> The fastest way to improve onboarding is not more docs. It is better answer retrieval.

> The fastest way to improve nurture emails is not shorter copy. It is sharper objection matching.

> The fastest way to improve sales coaching is not more call reviews. It is better pattern detection.

## Subheads

Subheads help scanners understand the argument. They should carry meaning.

Weak subheads:

- Features
- Benefits
- How it works
- Testimonials
- FAQ

Stronger subheads:

- See which client decisions are blocking the project.
- Turn every recorded demo into one coaching action.
- Start with one workflow before you roll out company-wide.
- Proof that the new process saves manager time.
- Questions teams ask before switching.

## Leads

The lead is the opening section after the headline. It should continue the thought, deepen relevance, and start the argument.

## Lead types and examples

### 1. Direct lead

Best for high-awareness readers.

Template:

> [Offer] helps [audience] get [outcome] by [mechanism].

Example:

> PipelineCoach helps sales managers turn recorded discovery calls into rep-specific coaching plans, so they can fix deal-stalling patterns without listening to every call manually.

### 2. Problem lead

Best when the reader feels pain.

Template:

> If [current behavior/situation], then [hidden cost/consequence].

Example:

> If your team answers the same onboarding questions every week, your documentation may not be the issue. The issue is that the right answer is too hard to find at the moment a new hire needs it.

### 3. Story lead

Best when the reader needs to see themselves.

Example:

> On Monday morning, the agency had final approval. By Wednesday, nobody could find it. The email thread had 47 replies, the designer was working from a Slack comment, and the account manager was trying to remember which version the client meant. That is how a simple approval turns into a margin leak.

### 4. Proof lead

Best when skepticism is high.

Example:

> After mapping approvals in one shared workflow, a 24-person agency cut approval-chasing messages from 31 to 9 on its next launch project.

### 5. Secret/mechanism lead

Best for sophisticated markets.

Example:

> The best sales managers are not reviewing more calls. They are finding repeated coaching patterns faster: missing next steps, weak discovery, unhandled objections, and deals with no economic buyer.

### 6. Contrarian lead

Best when common advice is stale.

Example:

> More content will not fix a weak point of view. If your posts do not make a buyer think, “This person understands my problem differently,” publishing daily only spreads the vagueness faster.

### 7. News/trend lead

Best when a market shift creates urgency.

Example:

> Buyers are entering sales calls with more information and less patience. That means your follow-up email cannot simply “recap the conversation.” It has to advance the decision.

### 8. Invitation lead

Best for workshops, webinars, templates, lead magnets.

Example:

> Bring one landing page. In 60 minutes, you will identify the promise, proof, and CTA gaps that are keeping qualified buyers from taking the next step.

### 9. Quiz/diagnostic lead

Best for self-assessment offers.

Example:

> Open your homepage and answer three questions: who is it for, what changes after they use it, and why should they believe you? If any answer takes more than five seconds to find, your page is making buyers work too hard.

## Sales argument structures

### AIDA

- Attention.
- Interest.
- Desire.
- Action.

Example:

> Attention: Your best leads may not need more emails.
>
> Interest: They may need a different argument based on the objection they still have.
>
> Desire: Our lifecycle map groups leads by price, timing, trust, and fit signals, then sends the next most useful message.
>
> Action: Download the 4-objection email map.

### PAS

- Problem.
- Agitation.
- Solution.

Use carefully. Agitate reality, not invented fear.

Example:

> Problem: Your onboarding answers live in too many places.
>
> Agitation: Every repeated question interrupts a senior teammate and teaches new hires that asking is faster than searching.
>
> Solution: OnboardBase turns scattered docs into role-based answer paths for the first 30 days.

### PASTOR

- Problem.
- Amplify.
- Story/solution.
- Transformation/testimony.
- Offer.
- Response.

Example outline:

> Problem: Client approvals disappear.
>
> Amplify: Revision loops eat margin.
>
> Story: A project stalled because nobody could find final approval.
>
> Transformation: Approval workflow reduced chasing.
>
> Offer: ApprovalDesk pilot.
>
> Response: Book a workflow review.

### Before-after-bridge

Example:

> Before: Every campaign report takes hours and still ends in debate.
>
> After: Every metric has an owner, a change, and a next decision.
>
> Bridge: DecisionBoard connects marketing performance to weekly action planning.

### Problem-mechanism-proof-offer

Use for direct-response pages.

Example:

> Problem: Demos stall because managers cannot spot coaching patterns fast enough.
>
> Mechanism: Score calls against your sales playbook.
>
> Proof: Teams see repeated objections and missing next steps by rep.
>
> Offer: Start with a 30-day pilot.

### Belief-shift structure

Use for thought leadership and sophisticated markets.

1. Common belief.
2. Why it is incomplete or wrong.
3. Better belief.
4. Implication.
5. Example.
6. CTA.

Example:

> Common belief: “We need more leads.”
>
> Problem: More leads only multiplies a weak sales argument.
>
> Better belief: You need the right argument for each stage of buyer doubt.
>
> Implication: Segment nurture by objection, not just persona.
>
> Example: Price-sensitive leads need ROI proof; trust-sensitive leads need case studies.
>
> CTA: Download the objection-based nurture map.

## Benefit writing

Features describe what it is. Benefits describe what the buyer gets. Deeper benefits describe why that matters.

### Feature-benefit-ladder

Feature:

> Call scoring.

Functional benefit:

> Managers can see which calls need coaching.

Business benefit:

> Fewer qualified deals stall after discovery.

Emotional benefit:

> Managers stop guessing which rep needs help.

Copy:

> Score every discovery call against your sales playbook, so managers know which deal-stalling behaviors to coach before the next pipeline review.

### Feature-to-benefit examples

Feature:

> Separate shoe compartment.

Benefit copy:

> Keep sneakers, laundry, and toiletries away from clean clothes in the main compartment.

Feature:

> CRM integration.

Benefit copy:

> See deal risks in the sales workflow your reps already use.

Feature:

> Templates.

Benefit copy:

> Start from proven structures instead of staring at a blank campaign doc.

Feature:

> Audit trail.

Benefit copy:

> Know exactly who approved the file, when they approved it, and what changed afterward.

## Specificity writing

Specificity improves credibility, memory, and usefulness.

### Replace vague words

Vague:

- better
- faster
- easier
- seamless
- powerful
- robust
- innovative
- optimize
- transform
- leverage
- comprehensive
- scalable

Replace with:

- exact workflow
- exact time
- exact audience
- exact result
- exact mechanism
- exact object
- exact before/after
- exact proof

Examples:

Vague:

> Make reporting easier.

Specific:

> Turn three spreadsheet exports into one Monday morning report that shows spend, booked meetings, and pipeline created by channel.

Vague:

> Improve customer experience.

Specific:

> Give support reps the customer's last order, open ticket, and refund history before they answer the chat.

## Curiosity

Curiosity is useful when it points toward value. It becomes clickbait when it hides the topic or overpromises.

Good curiosity:

> The overlooked metric that shows whether your nurture emails are building trust or just filling inboxes.

Bad curiosity:

> This one weird trick will explode your revenue.

### Curiosity + usefulness formula

> The [specific hidden/overlooked thing] that helps [audience] [valuable outcome].

Examples:

> The overlooked handoff note that helps CSMs prevent renewal surprises.

> The hidden homepage section that tells buyers whether your product is built for them.

> The small onboarding signal that shows whether new hires are actually confident.

## Proof placement

Place proof immediately after:

- A major promise.
- A surprising claim.
- A mechanism explanation.
- A price/value assertion.
- A guarantee.
- A comparison.
- An objection answer.

### Proof insertion example

Without proof:

> Our system helps managers coach better.

With proof:

> Our system helps managers coach the moments that actually stall deals: missing next steps, weak discovery, no economic buyer, and unresolved objections. In one pilot, managers found 47 coaching moments across 50 calls without listening to every recording end to end.

## Bullets

Bullets make value tangible and scannable.

### Bullet rules

- Each bullet should sell one meaningful idea.
- Start with the benefit or curiosity.
- Include mechanism or specificity.
- Avoid stacking vague nouns.
- Use rhythm and parallelism.
- Do not make unsupported claims.

### Bullet formulas

#### How-to bullet

> How to [outcome] without [tradeoff].

Example:

> How to write a useful follow-up email without sounding like you are “just checking in.”

#### Mistake bullet

> The [common mistake] that causes [negative outcome].

Example:

> The nurture mistake that makes interested leads feel like strangers to your sales team.

#### Hidden metric bullet

> The [hidden metric] that reveals [valuable insight].

Example:

> The handoff metric that reveals whether onboarding is creating confidence or dependence.

#### Mechanism bullet

> The [named mechanism] for [specific result].

Example:

> The 3-Approval Map for finding where client work stalls before the revision round starts.

#### Contrast bullet

> Why [common tactic] fails when [condition], and what to do instead.

Example:

> Why more sales call reviews fail when managers lack a scoring pattern, and what to track instead.

#### Artifact bullet

> The exact [asset] we use to [outcome].

Example:

> The exact message map we use to turn customer interviews into homepage sections.

### Weak-to-strong bullet rewrites

Weak:

> Includes email templates.

Strong:

> Five follow-up templates matched to the buyer's real objection: price, timing, trust, fit, or internal approval.

Weak:

> Weekly reports.

Strong:

> A Monday report that shows which accounts have unresolved blockers before CSMs walk into renewal calls.

Weak:

> Community access.

Strong:

> A private critique room where you can post one draft a week and get feedback on clarity, proof, and CTA before publishing.

## Objection copy

Objection copy should be direct, respectful, and specific.

### FAQ structure

Question:

> Do we need to replace our existing tool?

Answer:

> No. This sits beside your project management tool. It captures client approval decisions that usually get buried in email, Slack, and comments, then links them back to the work your team already tracks.

Question:

> How much setup is required?

Answer:

> Most teams start with one workflow. We import the active project, invite the core team, and map the first approval path during onboarding.

Question:

> What if our team does not adopt it?

Answer:

> That is why the pilot starts with one live project and includes adoption reporting. You will see whether the workflow is being used before expanding.

## Email copywriting

Email is a relationship channel. Respect the inbox. Write like a useful person with a reason to be there.

### Email principles

- One email, one job.
- Subject line earns the open; first line earns the read.
- Avoid fake personalization.
- Lead with relevance.
- Keep paragraphs short.
- Use concrete CTAs.
- Segment by awareness and behavior.
- Make the email forwardable when B2B.
- Do not hide the point too long.

### Subject line types

Direct:

> New: the Homepage Clarity Sprint

Problem:

> The approval is somewhere. That is the problem.

Curiosity:

> The quiet reason demos stall after discovery

Proof:

> How one agency cut revision rounds by 38%

Invitation:

> Bring one landing page to tomorrow's teardown

Contrarian:

> Your leads may not need more nurturing

Deadline:

> Enrollment closes Tuesday

Personal/forwardable:

> Worth reviewing before Q4 planning

### Email opening lines

Weak:

> I hope this email finds you well.

Stronger:

> Most renewal surprises were mentioned on a customer call weeks earlier.

Weak:

> We are excited to announce...

Stronger:

> If your homepage still explains features before buyers understand the problem, this sprint is for you.

```markdown
Subject: [specific problem/outcome]

Hi {{first_name}},

[Explain what is available and why it matters now.]

[Mechanism or proof.]

[Offer details in plain language.]

[CTA]

```

## Landing page copywriting

A landing page should match the traffic source and lead the reader through one decision.

### Landing page sections

1. Hero.
2. Problem.
3. Desired future.
4. Mechanism.
5. Benefits/use cases.
6. Proof.
7. Offer.
8. Objections/FAQ.
9. CTA.

### Hero formula

> Headline: [specific outcome or problem]
>
> Subhead: [audience + mechanism + benefit]
>
> CTA: [specific action]
>
> Proof/risk line: [credible support]

Example:

> **Find the renewal risks your call notes are hiding**
>
> RenewNote scans customer conversations for unresolved blockers, delayed next steps, and executive silence, then turns them into account-level action plans before renewal.
>
> [Book a renewal-risk demo]
>
> Connect your call recorder and review your first risk dashboard this week.

### Problem section example

> Health scores often drop after the risk has already appeared. The customer mentioned a blocker. The executive stopped joining calls. A next step slipped twice. But those signals lived in notes nobody had time to reread.

### Mechanism section example

> RenewNote looks for renewal risk signals across every recorded call: unresolved pain, missing stakeholders, delayed next steps, competitor mentions, and repeated support issues. Each signal is attached to the account, the call moment, and a recommended next action.

### Proof section example

> In a pilot with a 14-person CS team, managers identified 23 at-risk accounts that had no active escalation in the CRM. The most common signals were delayed next steps and unresolved implementation blockers.

### CTA section example

> See what renewal risk detection looks like on your workflow.
>
> [Book a 20-minute demo]
>
> We will show sample account signals and the first-week setup process.

## Sales pages

Sales pages need a fuller argument. Use when the reader needs education, trust, proof, or a complete offer explanation.

### Sales page flow

1. Headline: core promise.
2. Lead: problem, story, proof, or mechanism.
3. Stakes: what the problem costs.
4. Belief shift: why the old way fails.
5. New mechanism: why this works.
6. Product reveal: what the offer is.
7. Benefits: outcomes and use cases.
8. Deliverables: what they get.
9. Proof: case studies, demos, testimonials.
10. Offer stack: components.
11. Bonuses: relevant accelerators.
12. Guarantee: risk reversal.
13. Urgency: real deadline or capacity.
14. CTA.
15. FAQ.
16. Final close.

## Video sales letters and scripts

A VSL needs momentum, pattern interrupts, and a clear narrative argument.

### VSL structure

1. Hook: urgent problem, big promise, or surprising belief.
2. Credibility: why listen.
3. Problem expansion: what is really happening.
4. Failed old ways: why common solutions do not work.
5. New mechanism: the central insight.
6. Proof: story, demo, case study.
7. Offer reveal: what it is.
8. Benefits: what changes.
9. Stack: what is included.
10. Risk reversal.
11. Urgency.
12. CTA.
13. FAQ/objections.
14. Final close.

### VSL opening sample

> If your customer success team is surprised by churn, the warning signs probably appeared weeks ago.
>
> They were mentioned in a call, buried in a note, or skipped over because the account health score still looked fine.
>
> In the next few minutes, I will show you a different way to catch renewal risk: not by asking CSMs to write better notes, but by turning every customer conversation into a risk signal map.

### Transition phrases

- Here is why that matters.
- The old way breaks down here.
- That leads to the second problem.
- This is where the mechanism matters.
- Let me show you what this looks like.
- Now, this is not for everyone.

## Social copy

Social copy should create attention and transmit one useful idea.

### Social post structures

#### Contrarian post

```markdown
Most people think [common belief].

But [better diagnosis].

Here is what to do instead:
1. ...
2. ...
3. ...

[Optional CTA]
```

Example:

> Most teams think their onboarding docs are incomplete.
>
> Often, the docs are fine. The retrieval path is broken.
>
> New hires do not ask because the answer does not exist. They ask because they cannot find it when they need it.
>
> Fix that by mapping answers to moments:
> 1. First login.
> 2. First customer question.
> 3. First handoff.
> 4. First manager check-in.
>
> Documentation is not the asset. Findability is.

#### Story post

```markdown
[Specific moment]

[Problem]

[Insight]

[Lesson]

[CTA or question]
```

Example:

> A client once told me, “The approval exists. I just do not know where.”
>
> That sentence explained the entire margin problem.
>
> The agency did not need another project board. It needed a decision record.
>
> Every client approval should answer four questions:
> - Who approved it?
> - What version did they approve?
> - When did they approve it?
> - What changed next?
>
> If your team cannot answer those, the project is carrying approval debt.

#### Framework post

```markdown
Use this [number]-part framework to [outcome].

1. [Step]
2. [Step]
3. [Step]

Example: ...
```

Example:

> Use the 4P test before rewriting a homepage:
>
> 1. Person: who is it for?
> 2. Pain: what problem matters now?
> 3. Promise: what changes after they use it?
> 4. Proof: why should they believe you?
>
> If the hero section misses any of these, the page is making buyers work too hard.

## SEO copy

SEO copy must satisfy search intent before selling.

### Search intent types

- Informational: wants to learn.
- Commercial: comparing options.
- Transactional: ready to act.
- Navigational: looking for a specific brand/page.

### SEO intro formula

1. Answer the query quickly.
2. Define key terms.
3. Preview useful sections.
4. Mention who the guide is for.
5. Avoid long throat-clearing.

Example:

> Customer onboarding software helps teams guide new customers from purchase to first value with tasks, resources, milestones, and progress visibility. The best choice depends on whether your biggest bottleneck is implementation, education, handoffs, or customer accountability. This guide compares the main categories and shows what to look for before buying.

### SEO-to-conversion bridge

Do not force a hard sell too early. Use relevant CTAs.

Example:

> If you are evaluating onboarding tools because customers keep missing implementation milestones, download the milestone checklist we use to map the first 30 days.

## Editing lines

### Cut throat-clearing

Before:

> In today's fast-paced business environment, it is more important than ever for teams to collaborate effectively.

After:

> Client work slows down when approvals live in five places.

### Replace abstractions

Before:

> We empower teams to maximize efficiency.

After:

> We show teams which handoffs are stuck, who owns them, and what needs to happen next.

### Add stakes

Before:

> Our tool organizes customer notes.

After:

> Organize customer notes before renewal risks disappear into a call transcript nobody rereads.

### Add mechanism

Before:

> We help you write better emails.

After:

> We map each email to the buyer's remaining objection, so price-sensitive leads get ROI proof and trust-sensitive leads get customer stories.

### Improve CTA

Before:

> Learn more.

After:

> See the 4-objection email map.

## Rhythm and readability

### Rules

- One idea per sentence when stakes are high.
- Use short paragraphs for digital copy.
- Use subheads every 150-300 words on pages.
- Put the strongest words at the beginning and end.
- Avoid long noun stacks.
- Use active voice unless passive is more natural.
- Use repetition deliberately.
- Use contrast to create memory.

### Rhythm example

Flat:

> Our software helps managers review sales call data more efficiently and identify coaching opportunities that can improve team performance.

Better:

> Managers cannot review every call.
>
> But they can coach the patterns that keep costing deals: weak discovery, missing next steps, and objections that never get resolved.
>
> PipelineCoach finds those patterns automatically.

## Copy patterns by industry

### B2B SaaS

Emphasize:

- Business problem.
- Workflow fit.
- Integration.
- Time-to-value.
- ROI.
- Security/compliance.
- Stakeholder proof.

Example:

> Connect your CRM and call recorder. See which opportunities are missing next steps, economic buyers, or unresolved objections before the pipeline review.

### Ecommerce

Emphasize:

- Use case.
- Sensory details.
- Practical benefits.
- Comparisons.
- Reviews.
- Shipping/returns.
- Fit and sizing.

Example:

> The wide zip opening lets you see every item at once, so you do not have to unpack the bag to find your charger.

### Coaching/courses

Emphasize:

- Transformation.
- Who it is for/not for.
- Curriculum.
- Support.
- Practice.
- Proof.
- Identity and confidence.

Example:

> Build a posting system around your client stories, not generic prompts, so your content sounds like your expertise instead of a content calendar.

### Local services

Emphasize:

- Location.
- Trust.
- Speed.
- Process.
- Pricing clarity.
- Reviews.
- Guarantees.

Example:

> We inspect the leak, show you photos, explain the repair options, and apply the inspection fee to any approved work.

### Nonprofit/fundraising

Emphasize:

- Specific impact.
- Human story.
- Urgency.
- Trust and stewardship.
- Donor role.

Example:

> $42 provides one night of safe shelter, dinner, and a caseworker check-in for a family arriving after hours.

Do not exploit dignity or fabricate impact.

## Swipe creation without copying

Study great copy for structure, not theft.

When analyzing a swipe, extract:

- Audience.
- Awareness stage.
- Hook type.
- Promise.
- Mechanism.
- Proof.
- Offer.
- Objections.
- CTA.
- Rhythm.
- Emotional arc.

Then write a new structure in your own words for the user's product.

Example structural extraction:

> Headline uses specific desired outcome + surprising mechanism. Lead diagnoses old way. Body shows three proof points. Offer reduces risk with pilot.

New copy can use the structure, not the original language.

## Final craft checklist

Before delivering copy, ask:

- Is the first line strong enough?
- Is the audience named or implied clearly?
- Is the promise concrete?
- Is the mechanism believable?
- Is proof near claims?
- Are objections handled before the CTA?
- Is the CTA specific?
- Is there one primary action?
- Does the copy sound like a human wrote it?
- Can a distracted buyer understand it?
- Is every sentence earning its place?
