# 06 — Conversion QA, Editing Rubrics, Testing, Measurement, Compliance, and Anti-Patterns

This module helps the agent review, improve, and validate copy. It should be used after drafting and before publishing.

## QA mindset

Do not ask, “Do I like this copy?” Ask:

- Will the intended buyer understand it?
- Will they believe it?
- Will they care?
- Will they know what to do next?
- Does it reduce or increase buying friction?
- Does it match the channel and stage of awareness?
- Is it honest, specific, and supportable?

Copy QA is not proofreading. It is decision-path inspection.

## The 12-part copy scorecard

Score each item 1-5. Add notes and fixes.

| Dimension | 1 means | 5 means |
|---|---|---|
| Relevance | Reader cannot tell it is for them | Reader instantly sees fit |
| Clarity | Meaning is vague or delayed | Offer and value are clear quickly |
| Promise | Outcome is weak or generic | Outcome is specific and valuable |
| Mechanism | No reason to believe it works | Clear explanation of how it works |
| Proof | Claims unsupported | Major claims supported near point of doubt |
| Differentiation | Sounds like competitors | Meaningful difference is obvious |
| Offer | Deliverables/terms unclear | Offer feels tangible and compelling |
| Objections | Friction ignored | Key objections answered respectfully |
| CTA | Next step vague or mismatched | CTA is specific, visible, and appropriate |
| Voice | Generic or off-brand | Recognizable and channel-appropriate |
| Readability | Dense, abstract, hard to scan | Easy to skim and understand |
| Ethics/compliance | Risky, exaggerated, or deceptive | Honest, substantiated, transparent |

### Score interpretation

- 55-60: strong; polish and test.
- 45-54: usable but has clear improvement opportunities.
- 35-44: strategically weak; revise before publishing.
- Below 35: rebuild from brief, offer, or positioning.

Any score below 3 in clarity, proof, CTA, or ethics requires revision before delivery.

## Fast five-second test

For pages and ads, a distracted reader should answer:

1. What is this?
2. Who is it for?
3. What does it help me do?
4. Why should I believe or keep reading?
5. What should I do next?

### Example failure

> AI-powered growth platform for modern teams.

The reader cannot answer what it does, who it is for, or why it matters.

### Example pass

> Find the deal risks hiding in recorded sales calls.
>
> DealSignal scores discovery calls against your playbook and shows managers which reps need coaching before the next pipeline review.
>
> [See sample scorecard]

## Strategic QA checklist

Before line-editing, inspect strategy.

### Audience

- Is the audience specific?
- Does the copy distinguish buyer, user, influencer, and approver when needed?
- Does the reader recognize their situation?
- Are exclusions clear when helpful?

Fix examples:

Weak:

> Built for growing businesses.

Better:

> Built for B2B SaaS teams onboarding 20+ customers a month.

### Awareness stage

- Does the copy match what the reader already knows?
- Does it over-explain to most-aware buyers?
- Does it ask unaware buyers to act too soon?
- Does it introduce the category when needed?

Fix examples:

Most-aware CTA:

> Start your 14-day trial.

Problem-aware CTA:

> Download the workflow checklist.

Unaware hook:

> Your team may not have a documentation problem. It may have a findability problem.

### Market sophistication

- Has the market heard the promise before?
- Does the copy need a mechanism, proof, contrarian angle, or narrower audience?
- Are claims too familiar?

Fix examples:

Familiar:

> Save time with automation.

More sophisticated:

> Automate the three approval handoffs that slow every client project: brief signoff, revision decisions, and final delivery approval.

### Positioning

- Is the category clear?
- Is the frame useful or commoditizing?
- Does the differentiator matter to buyers?
- Does the copy explain why this instead of alternatives?

Fix examples:

Weak:

> All-in-one productivity platform.

Better:

> A client approval workflow for agencies whose project boards track tasks but lose decisions.

## Offer QA checklist

### Offer clarity

- What exactly is included?
- What happens after someone clicks?
- Is timeline clear?
- Is price or next step clear?
- Are limits or conditions transparent?

### Offer strength

- Is the desired outcome valuable?
- Is the offer specific enough?
- Is time-to-value visible?
- Is effort reduced?
- Is risk reversed?
- Is there a real reason to act now?

### Offer fixes

Weak:

> Book a consultation.

Better:

> Book a 20-minute homepage review. We will identify your top three clarity gaps and tell you whether the sprint is a fit.

Weak:

> Get access to the course.

Better:

> Join the four-week working program and leave with a client story bank, 30 content ideas, and 10 polished posts.

Weak:

> Start using our platform.

Better:

> Connect one call source and review your first renewal-risk dashboard this week.

## Proof QA checklist

### Claim-proof map

For every important claim, identify proof.

| Claim | Proof present? | Proof type | Location | Fix |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Reduces approval chasing | Partial | Case quote | Below fold | Add metric near promise |
| Easy setup | No | None | N/A | Add setup steps/screenshots |
| Built for agencies | Yes | Use cases | Hero/body | Add agency testimonial |

### Proof questions

- Which claim creates the most skepticism?
- Is proof close to that claim?
- Is proof specific or generic?
- Is proof relevant to this audience?
- Does proof include context?
- Are testimonials too vague?
- Are logos enough or should we show outcomes?
- Is there a demo or sample output?

### Proof upgrades

Generic logo strip:

> Trusted by leading teams.

Upgrade:

> Used by 430+ agencies, including 68 teams managing more than 20 active client projects at a time.

Generic testimonial:

> This saved us time.

Upgrade:

> “We cut approval-chasing messages from 31 to 9 on our next launch project because every decision had a version, timestamp, and owner.”

Generic claim:

> Easy to implement.

Upgrade:

> Start with one live project. We import the project, map the approval stages, and invite stakeholders during a 45-minute setup call.

## Clarity QA

### Clarity questions

- Is the first sentence understandable without context?
- Are there undefined terms?
- Are there too many ideas in one sentence?
- Are abstract nouns hiding concrete actions?
- Are buzzwords replacing specifics?
- Can a salesperson say this out loud naturally?
- Can a buyer repeat it internally?

### Clarity fixes

Replace jargon:

> Revenue orchestration infrastructure.

With buyer language:

> One workflow for tracking deal risks, next steps, and manager coaching.

Replace abstraction:

> Optimize cross-functional alignment.

With concrete action:

> Show sales, onboarding, and success teams the same customer handoff notes before kickoff.

Replace vague modifier:

> Seamless collaboration.

With observable result:

> Every comment, file, approval, and deadline stays attached to the client project.

## Readability QA

### Digital readability rules

- Short paragraphs.
- Meaningful subheads.
- Bullets for lists.
- Bold sparingly for key ideas.
- One CTA per section when possible.
- Avoid large text walls.
- Vary sentence length.
- Put important information early.
- Use concrete nouns and active verbs.

### Sentence simplification examples

Before:

> Our comprehensive platform enables organizations to streamline the management of customer-related operational workflows through advanced automation and real-time visibility.

After:

> Manage customer handoffs, next steps, and risk signals in one workflow your team can check before every renewal.

Before:

> By leveraging AI-driven insights, teams can enhance decision-making across complex revenue operations processes.

After:

> See which revenue decisions are stuck, who owns them, and what changed since the last meeting.

## Objection QA

### Objection inventory

Common objections by category:

B2B SaaS:

- Will my team use it?
- Will it integrate with our stack?
- Is it secure?
- How long does implementation take?
- How do we prove ROI?
- Can we start small?
- What happens to existing workflows?

Courses/coaching:

- Do I have time?
- Is this right for my level?
- Will I get feedback?
- Is it too advanced/basic?
- Can I implement it?
- What if I fall behind?

Ecommerce:

- Will it fit?
- Is the quality good?
- How fast does it ship?
- Can I return it?
- What size/color should I choose?
- Is it worth the price?

Services:

- What exactly do I get?
- How long will it take?
- What do you need from me?
- Have you done this before?
- What if I do not like it?
- Can you work with our team/tools?

### Objection response formula

> Acknowledge the concern. Clarify the condition. Explain the mechanism or term. Show proof or next step.

Example:

> You do not need to roll this out company-wide. Most teams start with one workflow, one team, and one success metric. If the pilot does not show adoption and useful data, you do not expand.

### CTA questions

- Is the CTA specific?

- Is it visible above the fold?
- Is it repeated after persuasive sections?
- Is there microcopy reducing anxiety?

- Does the CTA explain what happens next?

Better:

Weak:

Better:

## Email QA

### Email checklist

- Does the subject line earn attention without deception?
- Does the first line create relevance?
- Is there one main idea?
- Is the CTA clear?
- Is the email too long for its job?
- Does it sound like a person?
- Does it avoid fake intimacy?
- Is the reason for sending clear?
- Is segmentation considered?
- Does the email comply with opt-out requirements where applicable?

### Email rewrite example

Before:

> Hi there, we are excited to announce that our revolutionary platform has launched. It has many features that can help your business improve efficiency. Click here to learn more.

After:

> Subject: approvals should not disappear after the client says yes
>
> Client approvals often happen in the wrong place: email, Slack, comments, or a meeting note.
>
> ApprovalDesk gives each decision a version, stakeholder, timestamp, and next step, so your team can prove what was approved without chasing the thread.
>
> See the approval workflow here.

## Landing page QA

### Above-the-fold checklist

- Does the headline name a meaningful outcome or problem?
- Does the subhead explain audience and mechanism?
- Is there a clear CTA?
- Is there proof or risk reducer?
- Is the visual supporting the message?
- Could the page pass the five-second test?

### Section checklist

Problem:

- Is it specific and recognizable?
- Does it explain stakes?
- Does it avoid exaggeration?

Mechanism:

- Does it show how the product works?
- Does it avoid black-box claims?

Benefits:

- Are benefits tied to real workflows?
- Are they distinct from features?

Proof:

- Is proof specific?
- Is proof relevant to this audience?

FAQ:

- Does it answer real objections?
- Does it reduce buying anxiety?

CTA:

- Is the next step concrete?
- Is it repeated naturally?

## Sales page QA

### Long-form flow checklist

- Does the lead match awareness stage?
- Are stakes established before the solution reveal?
- Is the old way explained fairly?
- Is the new mechanism compelling?
- Is the offer stack tangible?
- Is proof distributed throughout?
- Are bonuses relevant?
- Is the guarantee believable and clear?
- Is urgency real?
- Are FAQs specific?
- Does the close restate value without sounding desperate?

### Sales page bloat check

Cut sections that:

- Repeat the same claim without new proof.
- Add generic hype.
- Introduce irrelevant benefits.
- Over-explain obvious concepts to high-awareness buyers.
- Use false scarcity.
- Distract from the main action.

## Ad QA

### Ad checklist

- Does it have one idea?
- Is the hook specific?
- Does it match the landing page?
- Is the claim compliant and supportable?
- Does the creative reinforce the copy?
- Does the CTA match traffic temperature?
- Is the audience clear?
- Are variants meaningfully different?

### Ad variant matrix

Test different angles, not only different words.

| Variant | Angle | Hook example |
|---|---|---|
| A | Pain | Your client approval is somewhere. That is the problem. |
| B | Mechanism | Capture version, stakeholder, timestamp, and next step in one workflow. |
| C | Proof | One agency cut approval-chasing messages from 31 to 9. |
| D | Contrarian | Your project board tracks tasks. It does not prove approvals. |
| E | Offer | Start with one live project before rolling out. |

## B2B sales enablement QA

### Sales asset checklist

- Can the champion forward it internally?
- Does it explain the business issue?
- Does it address each stakeholder's concern?
- Does it include proof and implementation plan?
- Does it clarify next step?
- Does it avoid marketing fluff?

### One-page business case structure

1. Problem.
2. Current cost or risk.
3. Proposed solution.
4. Pilot scope.
5. Success criteria.
6. Implementation requirements.
7. Decision needed.

Example:

> Problem: Approval decisions are scattered across email, Slack, and comments.
>
> Cost: Account teams spend time chasing decisions and risk revision loops.
>
> Solution: Pilot ApprovalDesk on one active project.
>
> Success criteria: reduce approval-chasing messages, capture final decisions, improve handoff clarity.
>
> Implementation: one setup call, one project import, client reviewers invited.
>
> Decision: approve 14-day pilot.

## Testing strategy

Testing should answer business questions, not satisfy curiosity.

### What to test first

High-impact variables:

1. Audience/segment.
2. Offer.
3. Promise.
4. Mechanism.
5. CTA commitment level.
6. Proof placement.
7. Page structure.
8. Channel/message match.

Lower-impact variables:

- Button color.
- Minor adjective changes.
- Tiny punctuation differences.

### Test hypothesis format

```markdown
We believe [change] will improve [metric] for [audience] because [research insight].
We will measure [metric] over [time/sample].
A successful result is [threshold].
```

Examples:

> We believe changing the CTA from “Book a demo” to “See sample scorecard” will improve conversion from cold paid traffic because visitors need proof before committing to a call. We will measure landing page CTA clicks over 2,000 visits.

> We believe leading with “approval workflow” instead of “project management” will improve demo quality because buyers already have project management tools and feel the pain around lost decisions. We will measure demo-to-opportunity rate for 30 days.

> We believe moving the case study above the feature section will increase scroll depth and demo clicks because the core promise is currently unsupported too long. We will measure scroll depth and CTA conversion over four weeks.

## A/B testing guardrails

- Test one strategic variable at a time when possible.
- Do not stop tests too early.
- Predefine success metrics.
- Segment results by traffic source.
- Watch downstream quality, not just clicks.
- Do not overfit small samples.
- Record learnings in a test log.

## Funnel diagnosis

### High traffic, low conversion

Possible issues:

- Wrong audience.
- Weak message match.
- Vague promise.
- CTA too high commitment.
- Insufficient proof.
- Page slow or confusing.
- Offer weak.

Fixes:

- Improve headline and subhead.
- Add proof above fold.
- Use lower-friction CTA.
- Segment landing pages.
- Strengthen offer.
- Match ad and page language.

### High clicks, low lead quality

Possible issues:

- Hook attracts curiosity but not buyers.
- Offer too broad.
- Lead magnet misaligned with purchase intent.
- Form qualification weak.
- Ad overpromises.

Fixes:

- Make audience more specific.
- Add qualification language.
- Change CTA from broad download to specific diagnostic.
- Use problem-specific proof.

### Many demos, low close

Possible issues:

- Wrong CTA for traffic.
- Copy overpromises.
- Sales and marketing mismatch.
- Pricing surprise.
- Missing stakeholder proof.
- Weak qualification.

Fixes:

- Add fit/exclusion copy.
- Clarify pricing or starting scope.
- Add business case content.
- Create stakeholder-specific proof.
- Align sales talk track with page promise.

### Trial starts, low activation

Possible issues:

- CTA attracts unready users.
- Onboarding unclear.
- First value not obvious.
- Product requires setup not explained.
- Wrong success metric.

Fixes:

- Add guided setup.
- Clarify first action.
- Use onboarding emails.
- Add empty-state copy.
- Change trial promise to first milestone.

## Compliance and ethics

This skill must produce honest marketing. In regulated or sensitive categories, be especially careful.

### Universal rules

Do not invent:

- Testimonials.
- Results.
- Customer logos.
- Credentials.
- Scarcity.
- Urgency.
- Guarantees.
- Scientific claims.
- Pricing terms.

Do not imply certainty where outcomes vary.

Use conditional language when appropriate:

- may
- can
- designed to
- helps
- typical
- depending on
- based on
- in this example

### Risky claim types

- Medical/health claims.
- Financial/investment claims.
- Legal claims.
- Income claims.
- Weight-loss claims.
- Security/compliance claims.
- Safety claims.
- Environmental claims.
- Comparative superiority claims.

### Safer claim rewrites

Risky:

> Guaranteed to double your revenue.

Safer:

> Designed to identify the funnel leaks most likely to limit revenue growth. Results depend on traffic, offer, sales process, and implementation.

Risky:

> Cure insomnia naturally.

Safer:

> Build a calmer bedtime routine that may help you fall asleep more consistently. Not medical advice.

Risky:

> The safest platform on the market.

Safer:

> Built with role-based access, audit logs, and encryption in transit and at rest. Security documentation is available for review.

Risky:

> Make $10,000 in 30 days.

Safer:

> Learn the outreach, offer, and sales-process fundamentals used by independent consultants to build a pipeline. Income outcomes vary and are not guaranteed.

## Dark-pattern avoidance

Do not create copy that tricks users into actions.

Avoid:

- Hidden fees.
- Confusing cancellation.
- Pre-checked consent boxes.
- Fake countdowns.
- Shame-based opt-outs.
- Misleading button labels.
- Obscured terms.
- Forced continuity without clear disclosure.

Better opt-out copy:

> No thanks, I do not need the checklist right now.

Avoid:

> No thanks, I prefer losing money.

## Accessibility and inclusivity QA

- Use plain language.
- Avoid unnecessary idioms for global audiences.
- Do not rely only on color to convey meaning.
- Use descriptive link text.
- Avoid stereotypes.
- Use inclusive examples.
- Make error messages helpful.
- Explain acronyms.
- Keep forms clear.

Examples:

Weak link:

> Click here.

Better:

> Download the onboarding checklist.

Weak error:

> Invalid input.

Better:

> Please enter a valid email address, like name@company.com.

## Anti-pattern library

### 1. The everything sentence

Problem:

> One sentence tries to explain audience, product, features, outcomes, integrations, and vision.

Fix:

> Split into headline, subhead, proof line, and bullets.

Before:

> Our AI-powered workflow automation platform helps teams collaborate, streamline, optimize, and scale complex business operations with seamless integrations and analytics.

After:

> Find the approval handoffs slowing client work.
>
> Map decisions, owners, timestamps, and next steps in one workflow your team can use beside its existing project board.

### 2. The adjective pile

Problem:

> powerful, seamless, innovative, robust, scalable.

Fix:

> Replace adjectives with observable details.

Before:

> A powerful, seamless dashboard.

After:

> A dashboard that shows which metric changed, who owns it, and what decision is due this week.

### 3. The fake insight

Problem:

> Saying obvious things as if they are profound.

Before:

> In business, communication is important.

After:

> Client work does not slow down because people forget to communicate. It slows down because decisions happen in places the project board cannot see.

### 4. The empty benefit

Problem:

> Benefit is too abstract to feel valuable.

Before:

> Improve efficiency.

After:

> Cut the three spreadsheet exports your team uses to prepare Monday's revenue report.

### 5. The unsupported superlative

Problem:

> Best, fastest, #1 without proof.

Before:

> The best tool for sales coaching.

After:

> Score discovery calls against your playbook and show managers which coaching patterns repeat by rep.

### 6. The generic testimonial

Problem:

> Praise without context.

Before:

> “Amazing platform.”

After:

> “We stopped asking which version the client approved because every decision now has a timestamp and owner.”

### 7. The CTA pile-up

Problem:

> Too many actions compete.

Before:

> Book a demo, read the blog, watch video, download report, contact sales.

After:

> Primary: Book a demo.
> Secondary: See sample report.

### 8. The wrong commitment level

Problem:

> Asking too much too early.

Before:

> Buy enterprise plan now.

After:

> Book a 30-minute security-ready demo.

### 9. The brand voice costume

Problem:

> Copy imitates trend language instead of brand personality.

Before:

> Crush your ops chaos with insane AI firepower.

After:

> See which operational handoffs are stuck before they slow the next deadline.

### 10. The buried lede

Problem:

> The most valuable point appears too late.

Fix:

> Move the core promise or proof to the top.

Before:

> Long intro about company mission, then result.

After:

> Lead with result, then explain mission if relevant.

## Revision workflow

Use this workflow to revise any asset.

### Pass 1: Strategy

- Who is it for?
- What stage?
- What objective?
- What offer?

### Pass 2: Structure

- Does the argument flow logically?
- Are sections in the right order?
- Is proof placed at the right moments?

### Pass 3: Specificity

- Replace vague words.
- Add concrete examples.
- Name workflows, numbers, timeframes.

### Pass 4: Proof

- Attach proof to claims.
- Add testimonials, demos, examples, or mechanism explanation.

### Pass 5: Objections

- Add FAQ or objection blocks.
- Reduce risk.

### Pass 6: CTA

- Clarify action.
- Add microcopy.
- Remove competing CTAs.

### Pass 7: Voice

- Align with brand.
- Remove off-voice phrases.
- Improve rhythm.

### Pass 8: Compliance

- Remove exaggerated claims.
- Add conditions/disclaimers.
- Verify proof.

## Line-editing checklist

For each sentence, ask:

- Does it advance the argument?
- Can it be shorter?
- Can it be more concrete?
- Does it repeat another line?
- Does it use the buyer's language?
- Does it create trust or reduce it?
- Does it belong in this section?

### Common cuts

Cut:

- “In today's fast-paced world...”
- “We are excited to announce...”
- “At [company], we believe...” when it delays the point.
- “Innovative,” “cutting-edge,” “game-changing” without proof.
- “Seamless” unless explained.
- “Unlock” when a concrete verb works.
- “Leverage” unless the audience naturally uses it.
- “Robust” unless specific.

## Final delivery checklist

Before delivering final copy to the user:

- Include assumptions if information was missing.
- Provide the copy in a clean format.
- Include variants when useful.
- Explain strategic rationale briefly.
- Identify proof gaps honestly.
- Suggest tests or next steps when useful.
- Do not claim unverifiable results.
- Keep the user's brand voice and constraints.
- Make the output immediately usable.

## Final QA rule

A draft is not finished when it sounds good. It is finished when the right buyer can understand it, believe it, care about it, and take the next step without unnecessary friction.
