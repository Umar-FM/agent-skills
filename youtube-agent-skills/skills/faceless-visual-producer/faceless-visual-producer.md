# Purpose

Give every important beat a visual job. Build a recognizable faceless visual language that proves claims, makes abstract ideas concrete, creates progression, and avoids generic stock-footage sameness.

## Use this skill when

- A locked script needs a storyboard or asset plan.
- A channel is overly dependent on generic stock clips.
- Custom graphics, maps, data, screens, demonstrations, or archival material are required.
- The visual system must become faster without becoming templated.
- A sleep video needs slow visual novelty without brightness spikes or startling cuts.

## Required inputs

- Script, retention map, and visual proof list.
- Channel visual grammar and brand standards.
- Asset budget, tools, team capability, and deadline.
- Rights and disclosure requirements.
- Thumbnail capture requests.

## Non-negotiable operating rules

- Visuals must clarify, prove, intensify, surprise, orient, or create emotion; decoration alone is insufficient.
- Original evidence and custom explanatory graphics outrank interchangeable B-roll.
- Maintain an asset-rights ledger from acquisition through final export.
- Synthetic visuals must not masquerade as documentary evidence.
- Reusable systems are encouraged; materially identical videos are not.
- For sleep content, vary visuals gently every 20-60 seconds rather than using energetic contrast resets.

# Workflow

1. Break the script into beats and assign each a visual purpose: evidence, orientation, mechanism, scale, character, progress, emotion, or reset.
2. Choose the strongest production mode per beat: original capture, screen recording, custom diagram, map, chart, animation, licensed archival clip, licensed stock, typography, or intentional negative space.
3. Prioritize original proof assets for the opening, major claims, turning points, and payoff.
4. Create a storyboard or animatic with shot duration, motion intent, source, rights basis, and transition logic.
5. Build recurring visual grammar: type hierarchy, map language, chart rules, transitions, iconography, textures, framing, and progress UI. Vary the substance and composition.
6. Design for comprehension: signaling, segmentation, limited simultaneous information, readable labels, and accurate scale.
7. Source external assets through approved channels and record rights, credits, expiry, and transformation in the asset manifest.
8. Coordinate thumbnail-specific original captures while sets, documents, data, or visual elements are available.
9. Produce assets with editable source files and consistent naming/versioning.
10. Review the assembled cut for visual monotony, unsupported claims, misleading simulation, and accessibility.

## Decision rules

- Use stock footage when it efficiently establishes setting or emotion, not as the main evidence for a unique claim.
- A simple custom diagram often creates more value than expensive generic animation.
- Do not animate every sentence; preserve focus and let important images breathe.
- Maps and charts must tell a story and maintain truthful axes, labels, and units.
- When rights are unclear, replace the asset rather than hoping it passes.

# Required outputs

- Storyboard/animatic and scene plan.
- Completed `shared/templates/asset_manifest.csv`.
- Original and licensed asset package with editable sources.
- Visual-system components and reuse notes.
- Thumbnail capture assets and disclosure notes.

# Quality gate

PASS only when:

- Every major claim and story turn has appropriate visual proof or intentional treatment.
- The opening and payoff have the strongest assets.
- External assets have documented rights and credits.
- Synthetic or illustrative scenes cannot reasonably be mistaken for real evidence without labeling.
- The video looks like this channel, not a stock library template.

# Handoff

Deliver organized assets and manifest to `retention-video-editor`; send voice timing needs to `voice-sound-producer`; send rights uncertainties to `rights-policy-brand-safety`; deliver thumbnail captures to `thumbnail-art-director`.

## Metrics owned

- Percentage of runtime using original/custom explanatory visuals.
- Asset reuse savings without performance decline.
- Rights exception/error rate.
- Viewer comprehension and spike/dip behavior around visuals.
- Visual production hours per finished minute.

## Failure modes to prevent

- Matching every noun with literal stock footage.
- Using motion as noise rather than signaling.
- Creating misleading charts, reenactments, or synthetic “evidence.”
- Losing source provenance during editing.
- Building a visual template so rigid that every video feels mass-produced.
