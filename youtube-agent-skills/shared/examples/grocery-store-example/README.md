# Worked Example: “Why Grocery Stores Keep Moving Everything”

This example demonstrates artifact flow for a low-cost faceless entertainment-education video. It is illustrative, not a claim that the concept is currently underserved.

## Channel fit

Primary viewer: curious adults who enjoy understanding hidden systems in everyday life.

Repeated promise: reveal the invisible incentives shaping ordinary experiences through fast, visual, evidence-backed stories.

## Concept brief

Premise: Grocery stores rearrange layouts for reasons that go beyond making room for products, and those choices change what shoppers notice and buy.

Familiar: everyone experiences grocery-store layouts.

Unexpected: the layout is an evolving behavioral and operational system rather than a static map.

Payoff: a visual model showing the main forces behind layout changes and how to shop with less friction.

Retention runway:

1. A vivid before/after map.
2. The obvious explanation and why it is incomplete.
3. Operational constraints.
4. Shopper-attention mechanisms.
5. A real store example or documented planogram.
6. Practical takeaway and final model.

## Packaging hypotheses

A. Title: **Why Grocery Stores Keep Moving Everything**
Thumbnail: the same aisle item shown in two distant locations with a bold path between them.

B. Title: **The Hidden Logic of Every Grocery Store**
Thumbnail: a simplified store map with one highlighted “trap” route, clearly labeled as an explanatory diagram.

C. Title: **Your Grocery Store Is Designed Like This on Purpose**
Thumbnail: overhead cart path versus a direct path, with one dominant contrast.

Opening obligation: show a concrete layout change and the final visual model immediately; do not begin with the history of supermarkets.

## First 30 seconds

0-5: “Last month this coffee was here. Today it is across the store - and the reason is bigger than making you walk farther.” Show documented before/after locations.

5-15: Explain that stores balance selling, replenishment, vendor agreements, seasonality, and shopper attention; changing one zone changes the whole system.

15-30: Preview the final map and deliver one immediate example that disproves the simplest theory.

## Production approach

- Original narration.
- Custom floor-plan diagrams and animated paths.
- Licensed/store-approved footage or original capture.
- Publicly available planogram/retail research with claim ledger.
- No generic montage presented as proof of a specific store's motives.

## Postmortem hypothesis

Primary: a universally familiar experience plus a hidden-system explanation should support Home/Suggested discovery.

Guardrails: the first 30 seconds must retain viewers who expected consumer psychology without overstating intent; comments should indicate the final model felt useful rather than conspiratorial.
