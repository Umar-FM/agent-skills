# Purpose

Create a channel thesis strong enough to guide hundreds of decisions. Translate a broad topic into a precise audience promise, an ownable editorial point of view, a coherent portfolio of repeatable formats, and a business model that can scale without becoming generic.

## Use this skill when

- Launching a new channel or major channel pivot.
- The audience is unclear or returning viewers are weak.
- Video ideas perform individually but do not form a coherent library.
- A new niche, language, market, or revenue model is being considered.

## Required inputs

- Founder capabilities, interests, credibility, constraints, and anti-goals.
- Audience evidence and competitive analysis.
- Production budget, available tools, and desired publishing capacity.
- Revenue goals and acceptable time horizon.
- Policy and rights boundaries.

## Non-negotiable operating rules

- Choose a primary viewer, not “everyone.” Broad appeal comes from universal framing around a specific promise.
- Define the channel by repeated viewer value, not by a list of subjects.
- Faceless identity must include recognizable voice, worldview, visual grammar, and quality standard.
- Monetization fit is a design input, but never a reason to betray audience trust.
- Search and browse are separate acquisition lanes and may require separate formats.

# Workflow

1. Describe the primary viewer through jobs-to-be-done, viewing context, emotional needs, sophistication, alternatives, and commercial intent. Avoid unsupported demographic fiction.
2. Write a one-sentence repeated promise: “We help/let [viewer] experience [value] through [distinctive method].”
3. Select an emotional engine such as wonder, mastery, suspense, justice, ambition, relief, humor, or transformation.
4. Define three to five content pillars. For each, specify viewer value, target surface, proof style, production burden, monetization fit, and sequel potential.
5. Design a format portfolio with proven repeatability and materially varied substance. Include browse-led gateway formats and search-led evergreen formats where useful.
6. Specify the channel’s editorial moat: access, expertise, data, taste, visual system, speed, community, proprietary process, or unusually strong synthesis.
7. Create the faceless brand system: narrator persona, language level, humor boundaries, visual motifs, sound identity, sourcing style, and correction policy.
8. Map the monetization ladder from audience intent to ads/Premium, affiliates, sponsors, products, memberships, or licensing.
9. Set production constraints, quality floors, risk boundaries, and anti-goals.
10. Design the first ten-video thesis test with hypotheses, expected audience overlap, and success/failure criteria.

## Decision rules

- Reject a niche selected only for rumored CPM or low competition.
- Prefer a promise that can generate at least 50 credible ideas and several revenue paths.
- Most uploads should plausibly interest the same core audience; adjacent experiments need a stated bridge.
- Do not mix incompatible viewer intents on one channel merely because the topics are related.
- A format is strategic only when it is repeatable, differentiated, and economically feasible.

# Required outputs

- Completed `shared/templates/channel_thesis.md`.
- Content-pillar matrix and format portfolio.
- Browse/search acquisition map.
- Faceless brand and editorial standards.
- Monetization ladder and first ten-video test.

# Quality gate

PASS only when:

- A cold operator can identify the target viewer and repeated promise without additional explanation.
- Every content pillar serves the same core audience in a compatible way.
- The thesis explains why this channel should win beyond “better quality.”
- The production model can sustain the promised cadence and originality.
- The business has at least two plausible revenue layers and explicit anti-goals.

# Handoff

Send the locked thesis to `audience-market-researcher`, `trend-competitive-intelligence`, `idea-format-developer`, `monetization-revenue-architect`, and `production-operations-manager`. Reopen it only when multiple postmortems show a structural audience or economics problem.

## Metrics owned

- Returning viewers.
- Audience overlap across uploads.
- Views and watch time generated by each pillar.
- Subscriber conversion and next-video movement.
- Revenue and contribution by pillar.
- Percentage of ideas that fit the thesis without exceptions.

## Failure modes to prevent

- Writing a generic mission statement instead of decision rules.
- Confusing topic consistency with audience consistency.
- Designing a brand that depends on a visible host when the channel is faceless.
- Adding too many pillars to avoid making a choice.
- Treating the first thesis as permanent despite contrary evidence.
