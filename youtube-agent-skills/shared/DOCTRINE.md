# Shared Doctrine

## The business objective

Build an original, faceless media property that earns durable attention and converts that attention into diversified revenue. The operating system favors videos that are easy **relative to their value**, not videos that are generic, automated, or interchangeable.

## The click-watch-satisfaction model

A useful creator-side model is:

`Qualified Watch Time per Impression = Impressions CTR x Average View Duration`

This is a diagnostic heuristic, not YouTube's internal formula. It prevents agents from treating CTR and AVD as separate games. The native YouTube A/B tool selects title/thumbnail winners by watch time rather than CTR alone, reinforcing the need to judge packaging by the viewing it produces.

A more complete business model is:

`Expected Contribution per Impression = Qualified Watch Time per Impression x Monetization Value per Watch Minute - Variable Production Cost per Impression`

Do not force precision where data is sparse. Use the model to frame tradeoffs.

## The promise chain

Every video has five linked promises:

1. **Channel promise:** why this audience should repeatedly return.
2. **Concept promise:** why this specific idea matters.
3. **Packaging promise:** what the title and thumbnail imply.
4. **Opening promise:** immediate confirmation that the viewer clicked correctly.
5. **Payoff promise:** a conclusion, transformation, answer, result, or emotional resolution worth the time spent.

Break any link and growth becomes fragile.

## The five forms of retention

1. **Question retention:** the viewer needs an answer.
2. **Progress retention:** the viewer wants to see advancement toward a goal.
3. **Stakes retention:** something meaningful can be won, lost, exposed, or changed.
4. **Character retention:** the viewer cares what happens to a person, group, or point of view.
5. **Aesthetic retention:** the experience itself is pleasurable, surprising, funny, beautiful, or emotionally varied.

Strong videos combine at least two. Faceless educational videos often rely too heavily on question retention; add progress, stakes, and emotion.

## Faceless authorship standard

A faceless video is original when the viewer can perceive a specific creator behind it. Evidence of authorship can include:

- a distinctive thesis or conclusion;
- original research or synthesis;
- custom experiments, calculations, maps, diagrams, or datasets;
- recognizable narration and humor;
- original visual metaphors or animation;
- substantive commentary on licensed clips;
- a consistent editorial worldview;
- transparent sourcing and corrections.

A synthetic voice is not automatically disqualifying. Generic scripts, templated visuals, minimal variation, scraped material, or undifferentiated AI output create both audience and monetization risk.

## Production leverage hierarchy

Prefer leverage in this order:

1. Better idea selection.
2. Reusable research and visual systems.
3. Reusable formats with materially different substance.
4. Batching by cognitive task.
5. Automation of administrative work.
6. Outsourcing with clear quality gates.
7. AI assistance with human editorial control.

Do not begin by automating the creative judgment that differentiates the channel.

## Source hierarchy

For factual and policy claims, prefer:

1. Official platform documentation and primary records.
2. Original research papers, filings, datasets, and direct interviews.
3. Reputable reporting with named sources.
4. Specialist analysis with transparent methodology.
5. Creator/coaching advice and social posts as hypotheses to test.

Always label inference. Never fabricate a citation, quote, statistic, source, or firsthand experience.

## Global anti-patterns

- Starting production before a compelling package exists.
- Writing an essay and adding visuals afterward.
- Long logos, greetings, credentials, or history before the click is confirmed.
- Using pace to conceal weak structure.
- Opening loops without satisfying closure.
- Repeating title text verbatim in the thumbnail.
- Optimizing for universal benchmarks instead of channel and traffic-source baselines.
- Treating subscribers as the audience rather than the people most likely to value the next video.
- Creating a format so automated that individual videos are barely distinguishable.
- Choosing a high-CPM niche with no credible viewer promise or sponsor-product fit.
