# 04 — Asset Recipes and Example Copy

This module gives practical recipes and sample copy for common marketing assets. Examples use fictional products and should be adapted to the user's audience, proof, offer, and voice.

## How to use these recipes

For each asset:

1. Confirm the objective.
2. Identify the audience and awareness stage.
3. Choose the core angle.
4. Use the recipe as a structure, not a cage.
5. Replace all generic claims with proof, details, and brand language.
6. Run the final asset through the QA rubrics in `06_conversion_qa_rubrics.md`.

## 1. Homepage recipe

A homepage must orient mixed audiences quickly. It should explain the category, audience, promise, differentiation, proof, and paths to action.

### Homepage structure

1. Hero: clear promise and CTA.
2. Social proof or credibility.
3. Problem/opportunity.
4. Solution/mechanism.
5. Use cases or benefits.
6. How it works.
7. Proof/case studies.
8. Integrations/process/security, if relevant.
9. Persona or segment routing.
10. CTA.
11. FAQ or objection handling.

### Homepage sample: B2B SaaS

Product: DealSignal, a sales coaching platform.

#### Hero

> **Find the deal risks managers do not have time to hear**
>
> DealSignal scores recorded sales calls against your actual playbook, then shows managers which reps need coaching on discovery, next steps, objections, and economic buyer alignment.
>
> [Book a coaching workflow demo]
> [See sample scorecard]
>
> Connect your call recorder. Review your first coaching patterns this week.

#### Credibility strip

> Used by revenue teams coaching 10,000+ calls a month.

#### Problem

> Managers cannot listen to every call. So coaching often depends on the loudest deal, the most recent mistake, or whatever appears in the pipeline review. By then, the pattern has already cost opportunities.

#### New way

> DealSignal turns every call into structured coaching data. Instead of scanning transcripts, managers see the specific behaviors that stall deals and the reps who need help now.

#### Benefits

> **Coach the pattern, not the anecdote**
>
> See repeated gaps across calls: weak discovery, vague next steps, missing decision-makers, pricing anxiety, and unhandled objections.

> **Make pipeline reviews less mysterious**
>
> Every risk signal links back to the call moment, so managers can hear the context before coaching.

> **Help reps improve before the quarter is over**
>
> Turn call patterns into weekly coaching actions instead of end-of-quarter surprises.

#### How it works

1. Connect your call recorder and CRM.
2. Customize the scorecard to your sales playbook.
3. Review coaching patterns by rep, stage, and opportunity.
4. Send managers one weekly coaching plan.

#### Proof block

> In a 30-day pilot with a 37-rep team, managers identified 112 missed next-step moments and 46 opportunities with no clear economic buyer attached. The team used those patterns to revise discovery coaching before the next pipeline review.

#### CTA

> See what your first coaching dashboard could show.
>
> [Book a demo]
>
> No platform migration required for the pilot.

### Homepage rewrite pattern

Weak homepage hero:

> AI-powered revenue intelligence for modern sales teams.

Rewritten:

> Find the sales call patterns that stall qualified deals.
>
> Score every recorded discovery call against your playbook, then give managers the coaching actions that matter before the next pipeline review.

Why it is stronger:

- Names a specific problem.
- Replaces vague AI language with workflow.
- Shows the buyer and outcome.
- Suggests time-to-value.

## 5. Welcome email sequence recipe

Goal: build trust, orient the subscriber, and move them toward the next appropriate action.

### Sequence outline

1. Deliver the resource and set expectations.
2. Teach a useful belief shift.
3. Share a story or proof example.
4. Offer a diagnostic or next step.
5. Handle a common objection.

### Email 1: delivery

Subject: your homepage checklist

> Hi {{first_name}},
>
> Here is the 12-Point Homepage Clarity Checklist.
>
> Start with the hero section. If a buyer cannot answer “who is this for?” and “what changes after I use it?” within a few seconds, the rest of the page has to work too hard.
>
> Over the next few days, I will send a few short notes on the most common homepage problems we see: vague promises, buried proof, and CTAs that ask for too much too soon.
>
> Get the checklist here.

### Email 2: belief shift

Subject: your homepage may not need better words

> It may need a better argument.
>
> A lot of teams try to fix homepage performance by polishing sentences. But the real problem is usually strategic: the page does not make the buyer feel understood, explain the mechanism, or prove the promise.
>
> Before rewriting anything, answer five questions:
>
> 1. Who is this for?
> 2. What painful problem or desired outcome matters now?
> 3. What changes after they use it?
> 4. Why does your approach work?
> 5. What proof reduces doubt?
>
> If the page does not answer those, sharper adjectives will not save it.

### Email 3: story/proof

Subject: “AI platform” was the problem

> A team came to us with a homepage that said they were an “AI operations platform.”
>
> It sounded modern. It also sounded like 40 other tools.
>
> In customer calls, buyers kept mentioning the same pain: approvals were buried across Slack, email, and project boards. So we reframed the page around approval bottlenecks, not AI.
>
> The new hero became clear enough for sales to use in calls:
>
> “Find the approvals blocking client work before they turn into revision loops.”
>
> That is what customer language does. It replaces category fog with buyer reality.

### Email 4: offer

Subject: want us to review your homepage?

> If your homepage still feels vague after using the checklist, we can review it with you.
>
> In a 20-minute homepage review, we look at your hero section, proof placement, CTA, and FAQ. You leave with the top three fixes we would make first.
>
> Book a review here.
>
> If it is not the right next step, we will tell you.

### Email 5: objection

Subject: before you redesign the page

> A redesign will not fix an unclear message.
>
> Design can make a strong argument easier to absorb. But if the page does not know who it is for, what it promises, or why buyers should believe it, a new layout mostly makes the confusion prettier.
>
> Start with the message map. Then design around it.
>
> Here is the checklist again if you want to run the page through it this week.

## 7. Cold email recipe

Cold email must be relevant, concise, and respectful. The goal is usually a conversation, not a full sale.

### Cold email structure

1. Relevant reason for reaching out.
2. Problem/opportunity hypothesis.
3. Credible mechanism or proof.
4. Soft CTA.

### Bad cold email

> Hi, I hope this finds you well. We are a leading provider of innovative solutions that help companies like yours grow faster. Would you be interested in a call?

### Better cold email

Subject: approvals on client projects

> Hi {{first_name}},
>
> I noticed {{company}} manages a lot of client launch work across design and content.
>
> Agencies at that stage often lose time when final approvals live in email, Slack, and design comments instead of one project record.
>
> ApprovalDesk gives account teams one place to capture who approved what, when, and what changed next.
>
> Worth a quick look, or is approval tracking already handled well on your side?

### Cold email variants

Trigger-based:

> Congrats on the new enterprise client announcement. When agencies start handling larger accounts, approval trails usually become more important because more stakeholders review each asset.

> We help teams capture approvals without replacing their project management tool.

> Teams using both Slack and Asana often run into one annoying gap: tasks are tracked, but approval decisions still get buried in conversation.
>
> We built a lightweight approval record that links decisions back to the work.
>
> Is that a problem your team is dealing with?

Proof-based:

## 8. Sales follow-up recipe

A follow-up should advance the decision. Avoid “just checking in.”

### Follow-up structure

1. Reference the specific conversation.
2. Restate the business issue.
3. Provide useful next evidence.
4. Suggest a clear next step.

### Follow-up example

Subject: approval workflow from today's call

> Hi Maya,
>
> Good speaking today.
>
> The biggest issue I heard is that your project board tracks tasks, but the final client decision often lives somewhere else. That is what creates revision risk for the account team.
>
> I attached a sample approval map showing how agencies capture version, owner, timestamp, and next step in one workflow.
>
> Suggested next step: run one active project through the map and see whether it reduces approval chasing before expanding.
>
> Want to review that project together next Tuesday?

### Follow-up by objection

Price objection:

> The useful question is whether approval chasing is costing more than the workflow. If your team spends 8 hours a week clarifying decisions, the monthly cost is likely already visible in delivery margin. I attached a simple calculator using conservative assumptions.

Timing objection:

> If a full rollout is too much right now, start with one live project. That gives you evidence without asking the whole team to change process mid-quarter.

Trust objection:

> I attached two examples: one approval map and one pilot report. They show what you would see before making any broader commitment.

Fit objection:

> This works best when approvals are the bottleneck. If the real issue is resourcing or unclear scope, I would not recommend starting here.

## 14. FAQ recipe

FAQ should handle real objections, not filler.

### FAQ examples

Question:

> Is this for early-stage startups?

Answer:

> Usually not. The sprint works best when you have real customers, a live product, and enough sales conversations to know what buyers ask before buying. If you are still choosing a market, start with customer discovery first.

Question:

> Do you write the final designed page?

Answer:

> We write the strategy, page flow, and copy wireframe. Your designer can use the handoff notes to turn it into a final page. If you need design, we can recommend partners.

Question:

> What if we have multiple audiences?

Answer:

> We will identify the primary homepage audience and create routing copy for secondary audiences. If the audiences need completely different promises, we may recommend separate landing pages.

Question:

> Can we use this if our proof is weak?

Answer:

> Yes, but the deliverable will include proof-gap recommendations. We will not invent proof. We will show where proof is needed and how to collect it.

## 31. Rewrite examples

### Example A: vague SaaS copy

Original:

> Our innovative platform empowers teams to collaborate better and unlock productivity at scale.

Rewrite:

> Keep every client file, comment, approval, and deadline in one place so your agency can finish projects without another “which version did they approve?” meeting.

### Example B: weak course copy

Original:

> Learn everything you need to become a better marketer.

Rewrite:

> Build a repeatable campaign system from customer interviews, offer strategy, landing page copy, and email sequences—then launch one campaign before the course ends.

### Example C: generic ecommerce copy

Original:

> Our premium backpack is stylish and functional for modern life.

Rewrite:

> A work backpack that keeps your laptop, gym clothes, lunch, and chargers in separate zones—so you do not have to unpack the whole bag to find one cable.

### Example D: generic agency copy

Original:

> We help brands grow with strategic content.

Rewrite:

> We turn your customer stories, sales objections, and founder ideas into weekly content that makes buyers trust your expertise before the first call.

## Final asset rule

Every asset should make the next step feel obvious, safe, and worth taking. If an asset is not helping a specific reader make a specific decision, it is probably just content—not copy.
